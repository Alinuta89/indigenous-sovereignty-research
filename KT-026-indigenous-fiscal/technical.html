Key Findings

1. The fiscal architecture is itself a sovereignty constraint

The Crown-to-Nation fiscal system was designed to administer obligations, not to empower self-governance. ISC data systems (JPCMS, SAP, GCIMS) are not interconnected, governance funding meets only 30% of needs, and Nations cannot independently audit whether treaty obligations are being met. The system the analysis maps is not neutral infrastructure — it is a structural expression of Crown control over fiscal relationships that treaties frame as mutual obligations. Strong

2. Indigenous equity ownership represents a structural departure, not a comprehensive solution

AIOC has facilitated $720M in loan guarantees across 9 projects involving 43 First Nations and Metis communities. The Aii/Enbridge deal ($1.12B, 23 communities, 11.57% equity) is the largest Indigenous energy equity partnership in North American history. These are genuine institutional accomplishments. They do not address treaty implementation, fiscal self-governance, or the underlying authority question. They create equity participation within the existing system, not a transformation of the system itself. The portfolio carries energy transition risk: 6 of 7 detailed AIOC projects involve hydrocarbons. Strong

3. Treaty Confederacies are prior-authority parties asserting fiscal dimensions of treaty rights

Treaty 6 (1876), 7 (1877), and 8 (1899) all predate Alberta (1905). Two King’s Bench rulings — Sylvestre (December 2025) and Athabasca (May 2026) — have confirmed that treaty rights constrain provincial constitutional action. Treaty 8 has demanded 2% of resource royalties ($320–400M on Alberta’s $16–20B resource revenue). These fiscal claims are primary sovereign claims, not tools for or against separation. Strong

4. The provincial-Indigenous coalition is the analytically strongest negotiating position — and may be politically unachievable

Treaty Nations and Alberta share grievances with Ottawa: resource revenue extraction, jurisdictional overreach, inadequate self-determination. A combined negotiating position would add s. 35 constitutional protection and treaty rights to Alberta’s fiscal demands. The evidence says it is the strongest position available. The evidence also says the coalition faces three structural obstacles: competing claims on the same resource revenue dollar, a track record of provincial governments failing to honour commitments to Nations, and ideological incompatibility within the separatist base. Partnership and co-option are parallel analytical findings, not argument and caveat. Moderate

5. Data sovereignty is fiscal sovereignty at a different scale

The entity that controls the data about Crown-to-Nation fiscal flows controls the narrative about whether obligations are being met. ISC holds that data in fragmented, Crown-controlled systems. Nations cannot independently access their own fiscal data. The most developed Indigenous data governance institution in Canada (FNIGC) does not extend to the fiscal domain. This is not a technology problem. It is a governance problem expressed through technology. Strong

account_balance Crown-to-Nation Fiscal Architecture

Where $2.5–3B in Crown-to-Nation flows through Alberta actually goes, who controls it, and why the accounting is fragmented across ISC systems that were not designed to serve Nation-level fiscal self-governance.

gavel Framing Note

The fiscal flows analyzed here are treaty expressions, not program delivery. The Crown does not "provide" Nations with funding — it meets, partially meets, or fails to meet obligations arising from treaties and from the constitutional relationship codified in s. 91(24) and s. 35 of the Constitution Act, 1982.

3.1 Mapping the $2.5–3B Estimate

No single published source reports the total Crown-to-Nation fiscal flow through Alberta. The $2.5–3B annual estimate is derived from ISC planned spending for 2025–26 ($25.3B nationally, $26.7B in total budgetary authorities as of Q3) proportioned by Alberta’s 12.8% share of Canada’s registered Indian population (143,241 of 1,116,320 as of December 31, 2024). Literature Proxy

The proportioning carries distortions: Jordan’s Principle is demand-driven and concentrated in Manitoba; infrastructure spending is project-based; health spending varies by remoteness and regional cost structures. Adjusting for these factors produces a range of $2.08–2.81B from ISC alone, extending to $2.5–3.2B when CMHC housing, Infrastructure Canada, CIRNAC, ESDC, and Health Canada channels are included.

Federal vs Provincial Channels: The Decision-Point Architecture

Crown-to-Nation flows reach Nations through two distinct architectures, and the distinction matters for sovereignty. In federal channels, Nations are at least nominally the fiscal counterparty. In provincial channels, Nations are positioned as service recipients within a system that has no treaty relationship with them. The province is not a treaty party. Provincial funding to Nations is not a treaty expression; it is a policy choice that can be reversed without constitutional constraint. Strong

3.2 Jordan’s Principle: The Cost-Shift

Jordan’s Principle ensures First Nations children receive the services they need without being caught in federal-provincial jurisdictional disputes. It is named after Jordan River Anderson, a Cree child from Norway House Cree Nation who died in hospital in 2005, having spent more than two years there while governments disputed payment responsibility for his at-home care.

Cumulative spending has reached approximately $6.44B since 2016 with approximately 7.8 million approved requests. Approved requests grew 367% between 2021–22 and 2023–24. Confirmed

The May 2025 Restriction

ISC announced that it would no longer approve school-related Jordan’s Principle requests for students attending provincial schools. The immediate impact in Alberta: Strong

  • At least 450 full-time and part-time educational assistant positions eliminated province-wide
  • Northern Lights Public Schools: 105 FTE EAs lost
  • Sturgeon Public Schools: $1.9M in Jordan’s Principle funding cut; ~35 support staff eliminated
  • Nearly 40% of responding Alberta school divisions reported job losses
warning Sovereignty Dimension

The Jordan’s Principle cost-shift illustrates a structural feature of the Crown-to-Nation fiscal architecture. Jurisdictional boundaries are drawn by governments, not Nations. When the federal government redraws an eligibility line, Nations absorb the fiscal consequences without fiscal authority to respond. At no point in this process were Treaty 6, 7, or 8 Nations consulted about the fiscal implications for their children.

3.3 The Two-Net Accounting Problem

Crown-to-Nation fiscal flows through Alberta can be measured two ways, and the two measurements produce fundamentally different totals:

Net 1 (Geographic): All federal money spent within Alberta’s geographic boundaries on Indigenous programs. This asks: how much federal money flows through Alberta’s territory?

Net 2 (Provincial-fisc): The money that appears in Alberta’s fiscal accounting. This asks: how much money does Alberta account for?

The gap between Net 1 and Net 2 is estimated at $2.7B (derived from KT-024 analysis). Assumption The gap exists because federal spending on reserve land does not flow through provincial books; NIHB and Jordan’s Principle are nationally administered; CIRNAC treaty payments bypass provincial fiscal systems; and federal infrastructure on reserve is invisible to Alberta’s budget.

The two-net gap is not an accounting error. It reflects fundamentally different theories about whose money this is and whose territory it flows through. Any analysis that excludes Crown-to-Nation flows from Alberta’s fiscal relationship with Ottawa systematically undercounts the total federal presence. Any analysis that includes Crown-to-Nation flows as "Alberta revenue" misattributes treaty obligations as provincial fiscal assets. Moderate

lock Data Sovereignty Implication

Nations that cannot access their own fiscal data cannot audit the Crown’s performance against treaty obligations. The reporting burden flows in one direction: Nations submit 29,000+ non-financial reports annually to ISC, but ISC does not report to Nations on whether obligations are being met. The data fragmentation is not a technology problem. It is a governance problem expressed through technology.

The Governance Funding Gap

Governance funding represents approximately 3% of ISC expenditures. Most governmental organizations operate in the 10–15% range. Only 30% of First Nations governance needs are met by ISC funding. The Band Support Funding formula is 30 years old, was inadequate from the outset, and has not been adjusted for modern governance functions. An immediate tripling has been recommended. Strong

This creates a dependency loop: Nations cannot build governance capacity because governance is underfunded, and governance incapacity is then cited as a reason for Crown-maintained fiscal control. Moderate

Canada currently has 25 self-governing Indigenous governments receiving fiscal resources through the Collaborative Self-Government Fiscal Policy. These are recognized as "autonomous orders of government" and "partners in the evolving system of fiscal federalism." Confirmed

Additionally, 191 First Nations have signed New Fiscal Relationship 10-year grant agreements, which provide greater spending autonomy, reduced reporting burden, and annual escalation with a minimum 2% growth rate. Confirmed

Section 4 — Stream B

trending_up AIOC & Indigenous Equity Ownership

The Alberta Indigenous Opportunities Corporation — a provincial Crown corporation established in 2019 — has deployed over $720M in loan guarantees across nine projects involving 43 First Nations and Metis communities. The analysis frames AIOC as what it is: a provincial instrument that facilitates Indigenous equity participation. It is not an Indigenous institution. It creates a financial pathway within the existing system, not a transformation of the system itself. The same critical standard applied to ISC is applied here.

4.2 Athabasca Indigenous Investments

23 First Nations and Metis communities acquired an 11.57% non-operating interest in seven Enbridge-operated pipeline systems. The $1.12B valuation makes it the largest Indigenous energy equity partnership in North American history. No community capital was required — the acquisition was financed entirely through the AIOC-backed guarantee and project-level debt. Confirmed

Approximate distributions to date: $25M cumulative over three years (Project Rocket/Cascade Power), translating to roughly $150,000–$300,000 per community annually. Moderate

compare_arrows Equity Returns in Context

The scale comparison is important: ~$25M cumulative AIOC distributions versus $2.5–3B in annual Crown-to-Nation flows. Equity ownership is a supplement to, not a replacement for, treaty-based fiscal obligations. A community receiving $300,000 annually from Aii and $10M annually in ISC obligations has two revenue streams governed by different logic: one commercial, one treaty-based.

4.4 Energy Transition Risk

Six of seven publicly detailed AIOC projects involve hydrocarbons. The portfolio carries material energy transition risk. Moderate

  • Canadian oil production is forecast to peak in the mid-2030s (CER, Wood Mackenzie, S&P Global)
  • Under IEA’s Net Zero Emissions scenario, global oil demand declines to 25–50% of current levels by 2050
  • Pipeline utilization remains strong at 94.8% (H1 2025) under current conditions Confirmed
  • Near-term performance is not evidence of long-term viability under accelerated transition

National trends are shifting: 46% of Indigenous-involved deals in 2024–2026 involved solar projects. But AIOC’s existing portfolio is overwhelmingly carbon-intensive, and the Duchess Solar project ($5.2M) represents less than 1% of deployed guarantees.

The Equity Spectrum

Revenue-sharing is one model within a spectrum. Equity ownership is another — further along, but not at the destination: Moderate

  1. Capacity funding — grants for participation. No authority.
  2. Impact Benefit Agreements — one-time or term-limited payments. Money flows, authority does not.
  3. Revenue-sharing — ongoing share of project returns. Better than IBAs, but Nations are passive recipients.
  4. Equity ownership (AIOC/FNMPC model) — ownership stake, board representation, financial interest. Authority is limited to the project, not the territory.
  5. Fiscal self-determination — own-source revenue authority, taxation power, fiscal policy independence. This is the destination. No single project delivers it.

Section 5 — Stream C

groups Treaty 6/7/8 Confederacy Strategic Positioning

Treaty Confederacies as political actors — prior-authority parties with superior constitutional standing asserting the fiscal dimensions of treaty rights. The fiscal demands documented here predate and are independent of the separatist debate. They are ongoing assertions of treaty-based fiscal authority.

5.1 Confederacies as Political Actors

Treaty 6: Confederacy of Treaty No. 6 First Nations

16 member Nations across central Alberta, with additional signatories in Saskatchewan. Treaty 6 is the only numbered treaty that includes a "medicine chest" clause — a treaty commitment to health care that Treaty 6 Nations have consistently interpreted as a guarantee of comprehensive health services. Strong

The Confederacy has been developing a revenue-sharing model as an alternative to federal contribution agreements. In March 2026, Treaty 6 Grand Chief Standingontheroad met with King Charles III at Windsor Castle, reasserting that the treaty relationship is between Nations and the Crown, not between Nations and the province. Strong

Treaty 7: Blackfoot Confederacy, Tsuut’ina, Stoney Nakoda

Comprises three distinct political entities: the Blackfoot Confederacy (Siksika, Piikani/Piikani, and Kainai/Blood Tribe), Tsuut’ina Nation, and Stoney Nakoda (Bearspaw, Chiniki, Goodstoney). These Nations entered Treaty 7 as distinct political entities and maintain distinct governance. Strong

Blood Tribe led the legal challenge that produced the Athabasca ruling. The Blackfoot Confederacy issued a formal denunciation of Bill 54 (Sovereignty Act amendments).

Treaty 8: The Largest by Territory

Treaty 8 covers approximately 840,000 km² across northern Alberta, northeastern BC, northwestern Saskatchewan, and the southern NWT. It is the largest treaty by geographic area and the most economically significant in resource terms — covering the Athabasca oil sands, Peace River agricultural zone, and major pipeline corridors. Strong

"Alberta came after Treaty 8, that matters. The province is a tenant on this land, not the landlord." — Grand Chief Trevor Mercredi, Treaty 8 First Nations of Alberta

5.3 Court Victories and Constitutional Standing

Chief Electoral Officer of Alberta v Sylvestre (December 2025, ABKB 712): The Alberta Court of King’s Bench ruled that a separation referendum would "fundamentally contravene" Treaties 7 and 8. This is a first-instance ruling, not a Supreme Court of Canada precedent. The Crown-realm argument (that treaties would transfer to a successor state) exists in international law scholarship, though no established democracy has successfully seceded (base rate zero from KT-023). Strong

Athabasca Chipewyan First Nation v Alberta (May 2026, ABKB 375): The Court found that Alberta’s Chief Electoral Officer erred in law by failing to adequately consult affected First Nations before accepting a citizen initiative petition on separation. The ruling reinforces the duty to consult on any matter that could affect treaty rights. Strong

Civil Disobedience as Reserved Capacity

Treaty 8’s June 2026 statement that civil disobedience is "the last resort" signals escalation capacity. The spectrum runs from legal action (completed), to FPIC demands (issued), to civil disobedience (reserved). The inclusion of civil disobedience in public statements is deliberate. Strong

6.2 The Permitting-Acceleration Thesis

"If you have an Indigenous partner with an economic stake who wants the project to succeed, are they going to oppose it? Indigenous ownership can actually shorten permitting timelines." — Mark Podlasly (Nlaka’pamux Nation), CEO and Co-Founder, FNMPC

Evidence Limitations

  • No controlled comparison. No study compares permitting timelines with and without Indigenous equity participation while controlling for other variables. Strong

Section 7 — Stream E

database OCAP, Data Sovereignty & Fiscal Self-Determination

Data governance is fiscal governance. The same authority question — who decides? — expresses itself through data systems, through fiscal architecture, and through land governance. These are not three analogous domains. They are three scales of a single sovereignty question.

7.1 OCAP Principles in the Fiscal Domain

The OCAP principles (Ownership, Control, Access, Possession) were articulated by the First Nations Information Governance Centre in the context of health and social research data. Applied to the fiscal domain: Strong

OwnershipFN communities own their data collectivelyNations own the data about fiscal flows to/from their communitiesCrown holds fiscal data in Crown systems

key Critical Finding

FNIGC, the most developed Indigenous data governance institution in Canada, does not currently govern fiscal or financial data about Nations. Its mandate and surveys focus on health, education, employment, and early childhood. The fiscal domain — where the most consequential sovereignty questions play out in daily governance — has no Indigenous data governance infrastructure. This gap is itself a finding about where data sovereignty has and has not been asserted.

The CARE principles (Collective Benefit, Authority to Control, Responsibility, Ethics), developed by the Global Indigenous Data Alliance (GIDA), extend OCAP into international frameworks. CARE emphasizes that data governance should serve collective benefit — in the fiscal context, this means fiscal data should serve Nations’ capacity for self-governance, not the Crown’s capacity for administrative oversight. Strong

7.2 ISC Data Architecture as Sovereignty Constraint

ISC’s data architecture structurally violates OCAP Access and Possession principles in the fiscal domain. Nations submit data upward (29,000+ reports annually) but cannot independently access consolidated data about their own fiscal relationship with the Crown. The entity that possesses the data about Crown-to-Nation fiscal flows controls the narrative about whether treaty obligations are being met. Moderate

The one-directional accountability flow — Nations report to the Crown but the Crown does not report to Nations — is not a gap in reporting procedures. It reflects the underlying authority architecture: the Crown determines what counts as compliance, what counts as adequate spending, and what counts as successful programming. Nations are positioned as subjects of Crown data collection, not as sovereign entities conducting their own fiscal assessments.

Section 8 — Stream F

handshake Partnership vs Separation — The Alternative Coalition

The coalition that shared grievances with Ottawa would produce — and why separatists refuse to build it. The finding that the coalition represents the strongest negotiating position available is presented as an analytical conclusion, not a recommendation. Whether the coalition is politically achievable is a separate question, addressed with equal rigour. Partnership and co-option are parallel analytical findings presented with equal weight.

8.1 Shared Grievances with Ottawa

Treaty Nations and Alberta share a set of grievances with the federal government that, on the surface, appear structurally parallel. The overlaps are real but require careful mapping — apparent alignment on the grievance does not mean alignment on the remedy.

8.2 Structural Divergences: The Same-Dollar Problem

The core structural obstacle is that Treaty Nations and Alberta have competing claims on the same resource revenue dollar. Treaty 8’s 2% royalty demand ($320–400M annually) would come directly from Alberta’s provincial revenue. The province offers equity participation through AIOC — which generates Indigenous wealth without reducing provincial revenue. Strong

Three additional structural divergences:

  • Environmental standards. Some Treaty Nations oppose projects that other Nations (and the province) support. Environmental assessment is not a simple Indigenous-vs-industry question.
  • Constitutional authority. The separatist base does not view treaties as legitimate constraints on provincial sovereignty. Treaty Nations view treaties as the legal basis for provincial existence.
  • Track record. Provincial governments in Alberta have repeatedly failed to honour fiscal commitments to Nations. The trust deficit is evidence-based, not rhetorical.

8.3 The Coalition Model

A provincial-Indigenous negotiating coalition would combine: Moderate

  • Alberta’s fiscal grievances with Ottawa (equalization, jurisdictional overreach)
  • Treaty Nations’ s. 35 constitutional protection and treaty rights
  • Two King’s Bench rulings establishing that provincial constitutional action is constrained by treaty obligations
  • The economic weight of the FNMPC project pipeline ($45B)
  • A combined population and territorial claim that no other province can match

International Comparator: The Basque Economic Agreement

The Basque Country — a sub-state entity within Spain that collects its own taxes through a fiscal agreement (concierto económico) negotiated bilaterally with Madrid — provides the strongest institutional comparator. Basque fiscal autonomy was achieved through negotiation, not separation, and produces fiscal outcomes (GDP per capita, public service levels) that exceed the national average. Moderate

The comparison has limits: the Basque Country is a regional government, not a coalition of a provincial government with prior-authority treaty parties. The structural complexity is greater. But the principle — that bilateral fiscal negotiation within a federation can produce outcomes superior to both the status quo and separation — is directly relevant.

The Co-Option Risk

Partnership requires mutual benefit, shared authority, and veto power for both parties. Anything less is a consultation with better marketing. The track record of provincial governments honouring fiscal commitments to Nations is poor. Matthew Wildcat (Ermineskin Cree Nation, University of Alberta) characterizes this as requiring "Indigenous realism" — assessing the separatist movement’s actual power and intent rather than its rhetoric. balance Parallel Findings

The partnership coalition may be the analytically strongest negotiating position and simultaneously a co-option risk. These are not contradictory findings. The coalition is strong because it combines constitutional positions that neither party holds alone. It risks co-option because the power asymmetry between the province and Treaty Nations means "partnership" can function as a legitimation device for provincial interests. Both findings must be given equal weight.

Post-Separation Bilateral Negotiation

If separation proceeds, Treaty Nations would negotiate directly with a successor state from a position of constitutional strength. The Sylvestre ruling (first-instance, noting the Crown-realm argument exists) establishes that treaty rights survive provincial constitutional transformation. Under this scenario, the coalition argument becomes moot — the relationship becomes bilateral rather than trilateral. Nations would negotiate on treaty terms, not as coalition partners. Inferred

8.4 Coalition Fracture Scenarios

Three modeled failure modes: Moderate

Fracture Scenario 1: Revenue-Sharing Formula Disagreement

The coalition agrees to negotiate jointly with Ottawa but cannot agree on internal revenue distribution. Treaty 8’s 2% demand ($320–400M) would reduce Alberta’s resource revenue. Alberta insists on equity participation (AIOC model) rather than royalty sharing. The first internal negotiation deadlocks on the same-dollar problem.

Fracture Scenario 2: Environmental Standards Dispute

The coalition supports a major resource project. Some Treaty Nations within the coalition territory oppose the project on environmental grounds. The province and equity-holding Nations want to proceed. The coalition must choose between environmental commitment and economic alignment. FPIC requirements give opposing Nations a veto that equity-holding Nations and the province resist.

Fracture Scenario 3: Energy Transition Divergence

Under accelerated energy transition, pipeline equity positions decline in value. Treaty Nations with diversified economies adapt; Nations dependent on AIOC returns face fiscal pressure. The province, still dependent on hydrocarbon revenue, resists transition policies. Treaty Nations that want transition support and the province that wants transition delay are no longer aligned.

Integrated Finding 1: The Sovereignty-to-Revenue Pipeline

The six streams reveal a connected pipeline from sovereignty claim to fiscal flow: Treaty authority (Stream C) grounds the legal basis for fiscal claims → the Crown-to-Nation fiscal architecture (Stream A) partially meets those claims through a system designed for administration, not empowerment → equity ownership (Streams B and D) creates an alternative fiscal channel outside Crown control → data sovereignty (Stream E) determines whether Nations can independently verify whether the Crown is meeting its obligations → and the partnership coalition (Stream F) represents the political mechanism through which these claims could be advanced collectively.

The pipeline has a structural weakness at every joint. Treaty authority is judicially affirmed but not operationalized in fiscal architecture. The fiscal architecture is fragmented and Crown-controlled. Equity ownership is growing but concentrated in hydrocarbons and Crown-designed mechanisms. Data sovereignty has not been extended to the fiscal domain. And the coalition faces the same-dollar problem that makes its most powerful members (treaty parties and provincial government) competitors for the same resource revenue.

Integrated Finding 2: The Scale Mismatch

The fiscal scale of different mechanisms reveals a stark hierarchy:

MechanismAnnual ScaleAuthority Basis

Equity ownership is a meaningful development at the project level. At the fiscal architecture level, it is a supplement, not a substitute, for treaty-based fiscal obligations.

Integrated Finding 3: The Authority Question at Every Scale

Each stream addresses the same fundamental question — who decides? — at a different scale:

  • Land: Who has authority over treaty territory? (Stream C)
  • Revenue: Who has authority over resource revenue from that territory? (Streams A, F)
  • Infrastructure: Who has authority over infrastructure on that territory? (Streams B, D)
  • Data: Who has authority over the data about all of the above? (Stream E)

The answer to all four questions should be the same entity — the prior-authority treaty party. In practice, each question is answered by a different institutional architecture with different power dynamics. The fragmentation of the authority question across scales is itself a governance finding.

Section 10

shield Stress Test Conditions

The stress test identified five conditional scenarios that require further modeling before the analysis can receive an unconditional PASS. These conditions reflect genuine uncertainties in the evidence base, not deficiencies in the research.

Condition 1: AIOC Portfolio Performance Under Energy Transition

If energy transition accelerates beyond current forecasts and pipeline utilization declines materially, AIOC equity positions could underperform. The analysis presents this as a scenario, not a prediction. Model: what happens to community returns if pipeline throughput drops 20%, 40%, 60% from current levels? At what point does the AIOC model shift from success story to cautionary tale?

Condition 2: Jordan’s Principle Cost-Shift Escalation

If federal eligibility restrictions continue to expand, shifting $500M+ from federal to provincial/Nation budgets without corresponding fiscal authority, the cost-shift becomes a structural transfer of Crown obligations to entities without the fiscal architecture to absorb them. Model: at what cost-shift level do provincial school divisions require emergency funding? At what level do Nations begin redirecting program funding to education gaps?

Condition 3: Coalition Formation and First Revenue Dispute

If a provincial-Indigenous coalition forms and its first joint demand involves resource revenue sharing, how does the coalition handle the internal divergence between Treaty 8’s 2% royalty demand and the province’s preference for equity participation? The coalition’s viability depends on resolving the same-dollar problem before the first negotiating session.

Condition 4: ISC Data Modernization Outcome

If ISC modernizes its data systems and Nations gain full access to their own fiscal data, does this validate the OCAP argument (by demonstrating that access was the missing component) or does it reveal deeper structural issues (by showing that data access alone does not translate to fiscal authority)? The data sovereignty argument must hold under both outcomes.

Condition 5: Treaty Nation Fiscal Self-Governance Conflict

If a Treaty Nation exercises fiscal self-governance in a way that conflicts with provincial revenue expectations — for example, by asserting taxation authority over resource extraction on treaty territory — the constitutional question moves from abstract to concrete. The analysis must hold whether the conflict is resolved through negotiation, litigation, or political accommodation.

Section 11

visibility_off Limitations and Evidence Gaps

Structural Limitations

  1. Alberta-specific ISC spending data does not exist in published form. All Alberta estimates are derived from national data proportioned by population share. Actual spending may differ substantially due to program-specific allocation, regional cost structures, and demand-driven variation.
  2. Nation-level fiscal data is OCAP-restricted. This analysis uses aggregate data throughout. Where Nation-level data would strengthen a finding, the gap is noted and the limitation respected.
  3. ISC data fragmentation means ISC’s own data is unreliable for precise fiscal analysis. Figures cited from ISC carry the uncertainty margin created by JPCMS/SAP/GCIMS non-interconnection.
  4. The AIOC model is too new for long-term performance assessment. The oldest project (Cascade Power) is six years old. Long-term performance data does not exist because the long term has not arrived.
  5. The permitting-acceleration thesis lacks controlled evidence. Directional evidence supports the thesis, but no study isolates the effect of equity ownership from other variables.
  6. The $2.7B two-net gap is derived, not independently published. No authoritative reconciliation of geographic versus provincial-fisc measurement exists.

OCAP Compliance Gaps

  • Community governance of AIOC equity returns is OCAP-restricted data. How communities manage their returns cannot be assessed from public sources.
  • Internal Confederacy dynamics are not publicly documented. This is appropriate — internal governance is internal data.
  • Jordan’s Principle Nation-level data (which Nations’ children receive which services) is held in JPCMS and is OCAP-restricted.
  • The fiscal modeling behind Treaty 8’s 2% demand and Treaty 6’s revenue-sharing model is not publicly available.

What This Analysis Cannot Answer

  • Whether the partnership coalition is politically achievable (the analysis assesses its analytical strength, not its political prospects)
  • How specific Nations govern their equity returns (OCAP-restricted)
  • The precise Jordan’s Principle spending by province (not publicly disaggregated)
  • Whether ISC’s data fragmentation is a feature or a bug of the Crown-to-Nation system (the analysis presents both interpretations)
  • The timeline for energy transition impacts on pipeline equity positions (scenario-dependent)
