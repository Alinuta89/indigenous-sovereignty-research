Indigenous Funding Network Resilience

1. The Fragility Finding

Alberta’s Indigenous funding network—42,095 government grant edges connecting 22 departments to 922 organizations—is catastrophically concentrated. 746 organizations (84%) receive the dominant share of their government funding from a single department. ISC alone provides $17.1 billion to 193 organizations.

The network has two structural features that amplify fragility:

  • GOA-breadth / ISC-depth split: Provincial ministries fund many organizations with small amounts ($30K average). Federal ISC funds fewer organizations with massive amounts ($88.5M average). Breadth funders create an illusion of diversification; depth funders drive the real fragility.
  • Funder-driven clustering: Organizations cluster by which government department funds them, not by geography or governance. ISC and CIRNAC are systemic risk nodes—their disruption would cascade through every treaty area simultaneously.

New risk: iGaming (launched July 2026, 2% to First Nations) may cannibalize physical casino revenue, reducing both the Host Nation 30% share and the FNDF 40% share.

4. The Clawback Problem

The central tension: If gaming and own-source revenue trigger federal transfer reductions, diversification is fiscally neutral or negative. Communities that succeed at diversification may be penalized by reduced federal funding. Communities that remain dependent maintain their full entitlement.

The interaction between own-source revenue and federal transfers is poorly documented, varies by agreement type, and is subject to political negotiation. Without clarity on clawback mechanics, every diversification recommendation is premised on an unknown.

Rural and remote Indigenous communities—particularly in Treaty 8—face severely constrained diversification options. Philanthropy is supplemental (estimated $15-30M/yr to Indigenous causes in Alberta—a fraction of government funding). Social enterprise requires startup capital, management capacity, and market access that remote communities lack. Fee-for-service faces mission tension in communities that cannot afford fees.

6. Immediate Priorities

  • Deploy the sustainability index to identify the 20-50 organizations at highest near-term cliff risk. Monitor distress signals quarterly.
  • Compile FNDF per-Nation data from the publicly available lookup tool and cross-reference with government grant data. This is the single most impactful data improvement.
  • Investigate clawback mechanics across agreement types before recommending diversification strategies.
  • Build actual cascade simulations from the T6 network data to replace the current inferred claims with computed results.
  • Run multi-year T3010 analysis (2018-2023) to establish trend baselines and identify organizations already in decline.

7. What This Analysis Does Not Claim

  • It does not claim that 84% of Indigenous organizations will fail. It claims that 84% are structurally vulnerable to a single government funder decision.
  • It does not claim that the colonial funding architecture should be preserved. The structural solution is fiscal sovereignty. The tools provided here are interim measures.
  • It does not claim that the T3010/network data captures the full fiscal picture. Gaming, own-source, and philanthropic revenue are invisible in this data.
  • It does not claim that cascade simulations predict reality. They reveal structural vulnerability, not destiny.
