Dependency Type Distinction

The index should distinguish between treaty-based dependency (ISC/CIRNAC = Crown fiscal obligations) and discretionary dependency (provincial grants = policy choices). A Treaty 6 First Nation with HHI = 1.0 because ISC provides its entire education budget is differently positioned from an urban charity with HHI = 1.0 because it received a single provincial arts grant. The policy response differs: the first is a treaty-implementation question; the second is a diversification question. STRONG

Profile of Highest-Risk Organizations

Organizations scoring CRITICAL (80-100) share these characteristics: INFERRED

  1. HHI > 0.9 (near-total single-funder dependency)
  2. Primary funder is ISC or a single provincial ministry
  3. Revenue declined 2021→2023 (post-COVID contraction)
  4. Essential service domain (health, child welfare, housing)
  5. Rural/remote location (no alternative providers or funders)
  6. Low reserve ratio (< 3 months operating expenses)

Treaty area prediction: Treaty 6 organizations (91.1% SFR) are overrepresented in the highest-risk category. MODERATE

MODERATE — Intra-category variation is substantial. Use as starting point, not conclusion.

The Clawback Problem: Every diversification pathway that generates own-source revenue risks triggering federal transfer reductions. If clawback is dollar-for-dollar, diversification is fiscally neutral. If partial, net benefit is reduced. If absent, diversification is additive. The clawback question is the single most important policy variable. It is poorly documented and varies by agreement type.

Successfully Diversified Organizations

Metis settlements show the lowest HHI in the network: Gift Lake (0.151), Buffalo Lake (0.176), Elizabeth (0.197). Their diversification reflects institutional design (Metis Settlements Act, Metis Settlement Accord) that creates multi-funder access by structure. Replicability for First Nations operating under the Indian Act is limited. STRONG
