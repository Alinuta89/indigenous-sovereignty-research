Indigenous Funding Network Resilience
Fragility, Cliffs, and Diversification Pathways in Alberta’s Indigenous Service Sector
KT-030 — Full Research Record (Tier 0) — — June 2026

This research concerns Treaties 6 (1876), 7 (1877), and 8 (1899) — agreements between the Crown and Blackfoot Confederacy (Siksika, Piikani, Kainai), Tsuut’ina Nation, Stoney Nakoda (Bearspaw, Chiniki, Goodstoney), Cree Nations (including Ermineskin, Samson, Louis Bull, Montana), Dene, and Chipewyan Nations. These treaties predate the Province of Alberta (1905). The funding networks analyzed here flow through treaty territory. Treaty Nations are prior-authority parties with sovereign claims, not stakeholders in a funding ecosystem.

1. Abstract

This paper analyzes the structural fragility of Alberta’s Indigenous funding network—42,095 government grant edges connecting 22 departments to 922 organizations. The central finding: 84% of organizations (746 of 887 with visible funding) depend on a single government funder for the majority of their grant revenue (Herfindahl-Hirschman Index > 0.6). This concentration risk means that a single budget decision, program sunset, or ministry restructuring can collapse services for entire communities. STRONG

The analysis reveals three structural features: (1) a GOA-breadth / ISC-depth split where provincial ministries fund many organizations with small amounts while Indigenous Services Canada funds fewer with massive amounts; (2) funder-driven clustering where organizations group by which government department funds them, not by geography or governance; and (3) ISC and CIRNAC as systemic risk nodes whose disruption would cascade through every treaty area simultaneously.

Critically, the network captures only government grants. An estimated $363 million or more in annual AGLC gaming revenue flows to First Nations through channels entirely outside this data. The First Nations Development Fund has allocated over $2 billion since 2006—a figure thirteen times larger than previously reported. Even with gaming revenue included, an estimated 80-82% of organizations remain single-funder dependent.

Treaty-area analysis reveals that Treaty 6 (central Alberta, including Edmonton) has the highest single-funder dependency (91.1%) despite proximity to the most diverse funder landscape. Treaty 8 (northern Alberta) has the lowest (65.9%)—possibly reflecting oil sands revenue that provides fiscal capacity outside the government grant network. Treaty 8 has zero Host First Nation casinos, structurally excluding northern communities from the largest gaming revenue streams.

The analysis produces three frameworks: a sustainability index measuring funding resilience across 8 dimensions, a funding cliff prediction model identifying organizations at highest near-term risk, and a diversification pathway assessment. The central tension underlying all diversification analysis is the clawback problem: if own-source revenue triggers federal transfer reductions, diversification may be fiscally neutral or negative.

2. Methodology and Data Sources

2.1 Network Data

The funding network was constructed in the Indigenous-Funding-Research repository (T6, agent: dr-morin, 2026-04-24). Edge sources:

  • Provincial grants: GOA ministry grant data, entity-resolved against dim_indigenous_org (81.4% match rate, Jaro-Winkler threshold 0.75). 17,445 edges from 31 GOA ministries/programs.
  • Federal transfers: ISC, CIRNAC, ESDC, and other federal department proactive disclosure data. 24,650 edges.
  • T3010 charity-to-charity flows: 2 identified flows totalling $29,255 (trivial; not included in edge set).

Total: 42,095 edges connecting 22 government funder nodes to 922 organization nodes (2,116 unique funder-org relationships). STRONG

2.2 Organization Identification

Organizations identified through T1 (CRA T3010 charity identification: 365 confirmed nationally, 52 in Alberta) and federal/provincial grant recipient matching. The 922 includes band councils, charities, Metis settlements, friendship centres, education authorities, and economic development corporations. Entity resolution used Jaro-Winkler similarity with auto-match threshold 0.92 and review threshold 0.75. Medium-confidence matches (0.75-0.92) have not been human-verified. STRONG

2.3 HHI Calculation

HHI = ∑(si)2 where si is the share of total visible government grant revenue from funder i. Computed for each organization from fact_funding_edge amounts. Organizations with HHI > 0.6 or a single funder providing > 80% of visible funding are classified as single-funder risk (SFR). STRONG

2.4 Community Detection

Louvain clustering (python-louvain, random_state=42, weighted by grant amounts). Result: 41 communities, of which 6 contain multiple organizations and 35 are single-org isolates. The mega-cluster (Community 1, 685 orgs) is driven by shared provincial funders. The ISC cluster (Community 11, 93 orgs) and CIRNAC cluster (Community 12, 29 orgs) reflect federal funder alignment. STRONG (with AP-13 caveat: different seeds produce different isolate counts; the 6 major clusters are robust)

2.5 Data Aperture

The network captures government grants only. It does not capture AGLC gaming revenue ($350-450M/yr), band council own-source revenue (T11: $2.13B across 35 bands), philanthropic grants ($15-30M/yr estimated), municipal grants, fee-for-service revenue, or impact benefit agreement revenue. Every finding in this paper carries this data aperture caveat. STRONG

3.3 The GOA-Breadth / ISC-Depth Split

Provincial funders reach more organizations with smaller amounts (GOA: 17,445 edges, $30K average). Federal funders reach fewer organizations with massive amounts (ISC: $88.5M average). This creates a structural split: provincial breadth funding produces low-value relationships that barely register in HHI calculations, while federal depth funding drives the concentration that makes organizations fragile. An organization may appear to have 10 funders while receiving 95% of its budget from ISC. STRONG

3.5 Non-Indigenous Comparison

The Canadian charity sector overall has approximately 49% government revenue dependency (Imagine Canada Sector Monitor). Estimated 40-60% of small charities (<$500K) have HHI > 0.5. The Indigenous sector’s 84% is substantially higher, driven by treaty-based funding concentration on ISC/CIRNAC and geographic isolation limiting funder alternatives. MODERATE

4. Cascade Modeling

Note: No cascade simulation code exists in the T6 codebase. All cascade results in this section are INFERRED from network structure. Formal cascade simulation is recommended as a follow-up.

4.1 Scenario: ISC 10% Cut

193 organizations lose 10% of ISC funding. For the estimated 100-130 organizations where ISC is >90% of revenue, this is a significant operational hit. Service domains most affected: child and family services, education, health. Geographic concentration: Treaty 6.

4.2 Scenario: Provincial Ministry Restructured

Alberta Indigenous Relations (161 recipients, $796.3M). Estimated 20-40 organizations face >30% revenue loss. Provincial restructurings typically result in program transfer (not elimination); impact is administrative disruption (6-12 months) rather than permanent funding loss.

4.3 Scenario: Federal Election Transition

6-12 month pause in new contribution agreement signing. 30-50 organizations with expiring agreements face funding gaps. Organizations with reserves survive; those operating month-to-month cannot. Estimated 3-5 potential closures if reserves are inadequate.

5. Funding Cliff Prediction

5.1 Risk-Scoring Model

Composite risk score combining HHI (25%), funder political risk (20%), agreement duration signal (15%), revenue trajectory (15%), service criticality (10%), reserves (10%), geographic isolation (5%). Score 0-100 where 80-100 is CRITICAL risk. MODERATE

5.2 High-Risk Profile

CRITICAL-scoring organizations share: HHI > 0.9, ISC or single provincial ministry as primary funder, declining revenue 2021→2023, essential service domain, rural/remote, low reserves. Treaty 6 organizations are overrepresented. INFERRED

5.3 High-Risk Windows

2026-2028 is the highest-risk period: post-COVID program sunsets completing, 2021-2023 contribution agreement cohort expiring (estimated 45-80 organizations), and provincial budget cycle sensitivity to oil prices. MODERATE

5.4 Early Warning Indicators

T3010-based: late filing (6-12 month lead), revenue decline >15% year-over-year (12-18 month lead), accumulated deficit (12-24 month lead), reduced program activity (6-12 month lead). MODERATE

Note on framing: When a funding cliff affects treaty-guaranteed services (education, health), it represents a potential treaty obligation failure, not merely a financial management problem. The language of “cliff risk” should not neutralize what may legally be a Crown breach of treaty commitments. STRONG

6.2 FNDF

Over $2 billion allocated since 2006 across more than 5,000 projects to 47 participating First Nations. $980M+ by 2016 (Alberta Auditor General). $159.2M in 2024-25 alone. Per-Nation annual allocations publicly available via alberta.ca lookup tool (2011-2025). STRONG

The prior research figure of “$147M allocated 2006-2019” was incorrect by more than 10 times. This error has been corrected (see Corrections Log, C-1).

Power dynamics: The 30/40/30 formula was set by the provincial government. First Nations did not design the revenue sharing arrangement. The 30% flowing to the Alberta Lottery Fund generates no direct benefit for First Nations. First Nations leaders have contested the equity of this formula. MODERATE

6.3 Two-Tier Gaming Economy

6 Host Nations (12.5% of Alberta First Nations) receive the 30% direct share (~$20M+ average per Nation). 42 non-Host Nations (87.5%) access FNDF only (application-based, ~$3.4M average). Disparity ratio: approximately 6:1 between average Host and non-Host allocations. MODERATE

6.4 Impact on the 84% Finding

If gaming revenue were included in HHI calculations: Host Nations would shift from SFR to non-SFR (6 orgs). FNDF-recipient Nations would see modest HHI reductions. Most organizations (charities, friendship centres, Metis) receive no gaming revenue. Revised SFR estimate: 80-82% — still catastrophically high. INFERRED

6.5 iGaming

iGaming Alberta Act (2025) launched the legal online gambling market July 13, 2026. 2% of gross iGaming revenue allocated to First Nations groups (separate from FNDF). First Nations leaders have raised revenue cannibalization concerns—iGaming may reduce physical casino traffic. The 2% allocation may not compensate. However, iGaming may also expand the total market rather than purely cannibalizing it. INFERRED

7. Diversification Pathways

7.1 The Central Tension: Clawback

The clawback problem is the single most important policy variable in diversification analysis. If own-source revenue triggers federal transfer reductions, diversification is fiscally neutral or negative. Communities that succeed at diversification may be penalized; those that remain dependent maintain full entitlement. The interaction between own-source revenue and federal transfers is poorly documented, varies by agreement type, and is subject to political negotiation. STRONG

7.2 Successfully Diversified Organizations

Metis settlements show the lowest HHI: Gift Lake (0.151, 15 funders), Buffalo Lake (0.176, 15 funders), Elizabeth (0.197, 14 funders). Their diversification reflects the Metis Settlements Act and Metis Settlement Accord—institutional design that creates multi-funder access by structure. Replicability for First Nations under the Indian Act is limited. STRONG

7.4 Comparators

Saskatchewan (SIGA): Indigenous-governed gaming authority. Revenue sharing: 50/25/25 (province/First Nations Trust/CDCs). More Indigenous control than Alberta’s AGLC-regulated model. MODERATE

British Columbia: New Relationship Trust ($100M endowment) for First Nations capacity building. More equitable gaming revenue sharing. BC Treaty Process provides some modern treaties with explicit fiscal arrangements. MODERATE

New Zealand (Maori authorities): Treaty settlements ($170M NZD) seeded self-sustaining $2B+ commercial portfolios (Waikato-Tainui, Ngai Tahu). Limited transferability due to different legal frameworks, but the principle—settlements seeding commercial independence—is relevant. MODERATE

8. Charity Sector Trends

8.1 Baseline (2023 T3010 Snapshot)

365 confirmed Indigenous-serving/led charities nationally. 52 in Alberta. Total revenue: $1,626.2M. Government dependency ratio (direct grants): 17.2% (floor estimate—intermediary government funding appears as donations). STRONG

8.2 The 52 vs 922 Gap

T3010 trend analysis covers only the registered charity subset (52 of 922 Alberta organizations, or 5.6%). Band councils, Metis settlements, tribal councils, and non-charity entities are excluded. Sector trends from T3010 data apply to charities only. STRONG

8.3 COVID Impact

COVID-era emergency programs (2020-2021) created the largest single-year funding injection in Indigenous organizational history ($1.8B nationally via ICSF). Post-COVID contraction (2022-2025) is creating funding cliffs for organizations that expanded on temporary funding. In real terms (CPI-adjusted, ~18-20% cumulative inflation 2018-2023), organizations whose nominal revenue returned to 2019 levels experienced a ~15-18% real decline. MODERATE

8.4 Multi-Year Analysis Status

Multi-year T3010 analysis (2018-2023) has not been run. T1 used a single-year snapshot. Running the analysis is a priority follow-up. STRONG (methodological gap)

9. Treaty-Area Network Resilience

9.1 The Treaty 6 Paradox

The largest sub-network (438 orgs) has the highest single-funder dependency (91.1%). Hypotheses: ISC scale dominance (massive multi-year agreements dwarf other sources), institutional path dependency (long-established ISC relationships), and population scale (larger populations = larger ISC per-capita transfers = higher HHI). MODERATE

9.2 The Treaty 8 Surprise

The most remote sub-network has the lowest HHI (0.796). Likely explanation: oil sands IBA revenue provides non-government fiscal capacity that reduces reliance on government grants. Treaty 8 also accesses resource-related government programs (Alberta Energy) that diversify the government funder mix. However, Treaty 8 has zero Host First Nation casinos, the fewest service organizations, and the most significant service coverage gaps. Lower HHI does not equal better service delivery. MODERATE

9.3 Funder-Driven Clustering

Louvain communities cross treaty boundaries. Community 1 (685 orgs) spans all treaty areas. Organizations cluster by funder, not by geography or governance. The network structure reflects colonial fiscal architecture—how government organizes its funding departments—not Indigenous governance structure. STRONG

9.4 No Geographic Hedge

ISC and CIRNAC are critical across all treaty areas. A federal policy change at ISC affects Treaty 6, 7, and 8 simultaneously. The funding network has no geographic diversification. STRONG

9.5 Sovereignty-Resilience Correlation

Suggestive but insufficient evidence. Metis settlements (with their own fiscal legislation) show the most diversified funding. Treaty 8 (with resource revenue providing more fiscal autonomy) shows lower government-grant concentration. But very few self-governing First Nations exist in Alberta, preventing a direct test. The correlation is a plausible hypothesis, not a confirmed finding. INFERRED

10. Sustainability Index

The sustainability index measures organizational funding resilience across 8 dimensions: revenue concentration (HHI), funder count, agreement duration, funder replaceability, own-source capacity, reserve adequacy, service domain criticality, and geographic isolation. MODERATE

Dependency Type Distinction

The index distinguishes treaty-based dependency (ISC/CIRNAC funding = Crown fiscal obligations under treaty) from discretionary dependency (provincial grants = policy choices). A First Nation with HHI = 1.0 from ISC is differently positioned than a charity with HHI = 1.0 from a provincial arts grant. The policy response differs: the first is a treaty-implementation question; the second is a diversification question.

Weighting Sensitivity

The index must be presented with sensitivity analysis. If changing weights from one reasonable scheme to another substantially changes the top-20 risk list, the index is not stable enough for individual organization assessment. It is a screening tool for identifying organizations warranting closer examination, not a definitive risk rating. STRONG

11. Structural Critique: Why “Resilience” Within Colonial Fiscal Architecture Is Contested

This analysis provides reformist tools—sustainability index, cliff prediction, diversification pathways—for navigating the existing funding system. Indigenous governance experts note that this framing assumes the colonial funding architecture is worth preserving. The counter-argument:

The contribution agreement model forces Nations to compete for resources that are owed by treaty right. “Diversification” within this system means finding more colonial funding sources. HHI measures dependency within the system; it does not measure whether the system should exist. The real resilience pathway is fiscal sovereignty—own-source revenue, treaty-based fiscal transfers, and the end of the grant-dependency model.

This critique is valid on its own terms. The analysis does not resolve the tension between reformist and structural approaches. It presents both: the tools for navigating the current system (sustainability index, cliff prediction) and the evidence that the current system is structurally flawed (84% concentration, funder-driven clustering that reflects colonial departmental structure, not Indigenous governance). STRONG

The structural solution is fiscal sovereignty: treaty-based block fiscal transfers, self-government fiscal arrangements, Nation-controlled revenue streams, and the First Nations Fiscal Management Act as an enabling framework. The tools this analysis provides are interim instruments. They are useful within the system. They are not the system Indigenous communities would design.

12. Limitations

  1. Data aperture. The network captures government grants only. Gaming ($350-450M/yr), own-source, philanthropic, and earned revenue are invisible. The 84% finding applies to government-grant dependency, not total fiscal dependency.
  2. Entity resolution uncertainty. 81.4% match rate; medium-confidence matches (0.75-0.92) not human-verified. Classification of “Indigenous-serving” relies on name/mandate matching. The 84% finding could be 76-92% depending on classification accuracy.
  3. No cascade simulation code. The “60%+ disconnect” claim is INFERRED. Formal cascade simulation is recommended.
  4. Community structure instability. 41 Louvain communities reflects the specific random seed (42). Only 6 contain multiple organizations. The 35 single-org isolates are an artifact of the algorithm, not a meaningful finding.
  5. Multi-year analysis not run. Time-series trends (2018-2023) are INFERRED from sector benchmarks, not computed from T3010 data.
  6. Treaty 7 data incomplete. Only 4 of ~7 Treaty 7 First Nations report FNFTA data. Tsuut’ina (major gaming community) is missing.
  7. Clawback mechanics undocumented. The interaction between own-source revenue and federal transfers is the most important policy variable and the least well-documented.
  8. OCAP compliance. This analysis uses publicly available data. No Nation-level data restricted by OCAP principles is used without verification of public availability. FOIP recommendations prioritize aggregate and administrative data over per-Nation fiscal detail.
  9. This analysis does not predict closures. It identifies structural vulnerability. Organizations scored as “high risk” may find replacement funders, merge, or receive emergency support. The model identifies organizations that should receive attention, not organizations that will fail.
