Research — KT-030
Technical Working Briefing Decision
Indigenous Funding Network Resilience
Decision-Maker Summary — June 2026

This research concerns Treaties 6, 7, and 8 — agreements between the Crown and sovereign First Nations predating Alberta (1905). Treaty Nations are prior-authority parties, not stakeholders.

The Problem

84% of Alberta’s 922 Indigenous organizations depend on a single government funder for the majority of their grant revenue. One budget decision, one program sunset, one ministry restructuring can collapse services for entire communities.

The funding network—42,095 government grant edges connecting 22 departments to 922 organizations—reveals catastrophic concentration. ISC alone provides $17.1 billion to 193 organizations. The top 5 government funders collectively reach nearly every organization in the network. If any one of them restructures, hundreds of organizations face immediate revenue loss.

This fragility is not organizational failure. It is the structural consequence of a colonial fiscal architecture that channels treaty obligations through competitive contribution agreements with defined terms, rather than stable fiscal transfers.

Treaty 6 (Edmonton region) is the most dependent despite being closest to the most diverse funder landscape. Treaty 8 (northern Alberta) has no casinos and the most service gaps, yet shows lower government-grant concentration—possibly because oil sands revenue provides non-government fiscal capacity.

Three Recommendations

1

Build the early warning system

Deploy the sustainability index and cliff prediction framework to identify the 20-50 organizations at highest near-term risk. Monitor T3010 distress signals (declining revenue, late filing, shrinking surplus) as leading indicators. Prioritize Treaty 6 organizations (91.1% single-funder dependent) and essential service domains (child welfare, health, housing).

2

Close the gaming revenue data gap

FNDF per-Nation allocation data is publicly available (alberta.ca lookup tool). Compile it. Cross-reference with government grant data to produce the first combined picture of Indigenous fiscal flows in Alberta. File targeted FOIP requests for FNDF category breakdowns. This is the single most impactful data improvement available.

3

Resolve the clawback question

Every diversification pathway (gaming, social enterprise, own-source) is undercut if federal transfers are reduced dollar-for-dollar when communities generate non-government revenue. Before advising diversification, determine whether clawback is operative, partial, or absent—and for which agreement types. Without this answer, diversification advice is premised on an unknown.

What This Analysis Does Not Do

It does not advocate for specific policy outcomes. It does not treat the colonial funding architecture as permanent or desirable. The structural solution to funding fragility is fiscal sovereignty—treaty-based block transfers, self-government fiscal arrangements, and Nation-controlled revenue. The tools this analysis provides (sustainability index, cliff prediction, diversification assessment) are interim instruments for navigating a system that Indigenous governance experts consider fundamentally flawed. They are useful within the system. They are not the system Indigenous communities would design.
