1. Abstract

This paper presents a deterministic, comparator-informed analysis of Alberta's sovereignty position within Canadian Confederation. Drawing on 120+ historical cases of sovereignty change since the Peace of Westphalia (1648), three independent economic analyses, two Alberta Court of King's Bench decisions, and the Supreme Court of Canada's Secession Reference (1998), the analysis examines whether Alberta's separation from Canada is constitutionally, economically, and historically viable.

The research originated from a Goa (India) sovereignty case study and expanded through global comparators—Scotland, Catalonia, Quebec, Ukraine, Western Australia, Flanders, and others—to expose a universal pattern: derivative entities that skip prior-authority consent produce permanent crises. Alberta's situation is historically unprecedented: no other democratic sovereignty movement has been blocked by a court on treaty-rights grounds. Strong

Six parallel research streams examine: (A) the chain of title from the Royal Proclamation of 1763 through Treaties 6, 7, and 8 to the Alberta Act of 1905; (B) a four-layer economic framework converging on $20–40B/year GDP losses from separation; (C) treaty rights, constitutional law, and the Sylvestre and ACFN court decisions; (D) a six-category global comparator matrix validating 10 cross-case findings; (E) BC's parallel crisis of unresolved Aboriginal title; and (F) constitutional evolution pathways that run through Indigenous consent rather than around it.

Dual-Track Presentation (ST-1) This analysis presents findings under two plausible Supreme Court of Canada outcomes. Track A: Sylvestre/ACFN affirmed—treaty rights as structural prohibition on separation. Track B: SCC reframes to process requirement—treaty rights must be substantively addressed during negotiations, but are not an absolute bar. The core findings—economic costs, comparator evidence, geographic impossibility, and base-rate finding—hold under both tracks. Where findings differ between tracks, both framings are presented.

The central empirical finding: no established democracy has fractured through unilateral secession. The base rate is zero. Strong Alberta's separation faces formidable legal, economic, and constitutional obstacles that have been established at the trial-court level (ABKB, under appeal) and are grounded in well-established constitutional principles, but which have not been tested at the SCC and could be reframed or narrowed on appeal.

The analysis's most consequential finding may be the one it did not set out to make: the legal architecture examined here—prior authority, chain of title, treaty rights as constitutional preconditions—has implications that extend far beyond the Alberta separation debate. Treaty nations may use these same legal principles to advance their own sovereignty claims, treaty modernization, resource co-management, and constitutional recognition. This possibility is not a side effect of the analysis—it is its most significant implication.

This analysis employs a multi-stream research swarm: six parallel policy-research streams, a landscape scan, cross-stream reconciliation, triple red-team review (separatist, constitutional skeptic, Indigenous sovereignty scholar), two-pass citation verification (190 citations, 83.7% verified), and a nine-scenario stress test. The scrutiny level is academic publication.

The economic analysis uses a four-layer deterministic framework: Layer 1 (static fiscal flows), Layer 2 (institutional replacement costs), Layer 3 (dynamic effects via synthetic controls), and Layer 4 (negotiation scenarios). 2.6 Non-Advocacy Posture

This is analysis, not argument. The evidence shows what it shows. The fiscal grievance driving Alberta's sovereignty movement is real and documented. The proposed remedy—separation—worsens Alberta's fiscal position by every measure examined. Both statements are findings, not advocacy positions. The analysis validates the grievance while demonstrating that the proposed remedy fails on legal, economic, and constitutional grounds.

Alberta's sovereignty claim fails on chain-of-title grounds. The province is a derivative entity, created by federal statute in 1905, the last link in a chain of authority that runs from the Royal Proclamation of 1763 through the treaties between the Crown and First Nations. At no point in that chain does Alberta acquire independent sovereignty. Strong

3.3 The "Proves Too Much" Objection

The chain-of-title argument's strongest critique is that it delegitimizes all provincial authority, not just Alberta's separation bid. If Alberta's authority is derivative and conditional, then so is every province's. The analysis offers three layers of response:

Layer 1: The operating-within/transcending distinction. All provinces exercise derivative authority within the constitutional framework. The chain-of-title problem activates at the point of rupture—when a province claims independent sovereignty outside the framework. This distinction is analytically defensible but judicially untested. Moderate

Layer 2: The SCC reframing prediction. The analysis predicts the SCC would apply the chain-of-title argument specifically to secession (where the framework would be ruptured) rather than to routine governance (where the framework remains intact). This is a prediction, not an account of current law. Inferred

Layer 3: The Indigenous perspective acknowledgment. The "proves too much" response works within settler-state constitutional law but would not necessarily be accepted by Indigenous legal orders where the delegation itself is contested. If treaties were sharing agreements—as Treaty nations have consistently maintained—then Crown sovereignty claims are fundamentally challenged across all treaty territories. The "proves too much" objection may be the argument's most consequential finding. Strong

Honest Limitation The chain-of-title argument as historical narrative rests on strong evidence. As legal doctrine, it is analytically sound but judicially untested above ABKB. A court might accept, reject, or reformulate the operating-within/transcending distinction. The analysis proceeds on the assumption that the distinction holds but acknowledges this as a structural vulnerability.

3.4 Treaty Nations' Sovereignty Framing

Treaty Nations' own sovereignty narratives offer a fundamentally different framing. Where settler-state constitutional law treats treaties as instruments of cession, many Treaty Nations understand them as sharing agreements—protocols for coexistence that preserved sovereign authority rather than surrendering it.2

"Alberta came after Treaty 8, that matters." — Grand Chief Trevor Mercredi, Treaty 8 First Nations of Alberta
"Treaties are not historical documents — they are living constitutional agreements." — Chief Samuel Crowfoot, Siksika Nation

If the sharing-agreement interpretation is correct, the chain-of-title analysis has implications that extend far beyond Alberta's separation debate. Crown sovereignty claims across all treaty territories would be fundamentally challenged. Indigenous legal orders predate the chain-of-title framework itself, and the prior-authority parties whose rights are at issue contest the framework's foundational premises. Strong

4. Economic Scenario Modeling

4.1 The Fiscal Grievance

Alberta's fiscal grievance is real. The province is a net federal contributor of approximately $14.5B/year (range: $12–21B), depending on methodology and year.3 Moderate–Strong This fiscal outflow is the economic core of the separatist argument. The analysis validates this grievance while examining whether separation is the remedy.

4.2 Four-Layer Framework

Layer 1: Static Fiscal Flows

Alberta contributes more in federal taxes than it receives in federal spending and transfers. The net outflow of approximately $14.5B/year reflects Alberta's above-average incomes and resource revenues. However, this outflow is a feature of the equalization system, not a defect: all above-average provinces are net contributors. Ontario and BC are also net contributors in most years. Moderate–Strong

Layer 2: Institutional Replacement Costs

An independent Alberta would need to build from scratch: central bank and currency system, military forces (NATO obligations estimated at $25B/year), border infrastructure, trade negotiation apparatus, regulatory bodies (CFIA, CRTC, OSFI equivalents), and OAS/CPP/EI/CTB replacement systems. The Smith government has estimated total transitional costs at approximately $400B, including approximately $170B in federal debt share.4 Moderate

Evidence Note The $400B figure is Smith's number, and she is positioned on the Remain side. The $10–15B annual benefit replacement sub-figure has not been confirmed as a specific Smith figure (discovery correction #9). The figure should be treated as an internally consistent political estimate, not an independently audited cost.

4.3 The APP Fiscal Plan

The Alberta Prosperity Project's "Value of Freedom" plan proposes scrapping income and sales taxes (eliminating approximately $80B in revenue), backing a new currency with oil, gold, and Bitcoin, and increasing oil production to 9.5 million barrels per day. Strong Tombe has described this as "fiscal fantasy."6 The IEA projects global oil demand peaking before 2030 in all scenarios, making the 9.5M bbl/day target unrealistic. The Alberta government's own FOIP response revealed zero cost-benefit analysis of separation had been conducted.7

4.4 Identity vs. Economics

Ipsos polling (January 2026) found 28% support for separation. When costs were surfaced, support dropped to 15–16%. Strong Angus Reid found only 8% support "regardless of consequences." Strong A June 2026 wave found support at 18%. Strong

The polling reveals that the separatist constituency is structurally threefold: an identity core (approximately 8%) that supports separation regardless of cost; a conditional middle (approximately 10–12%) that supports separation only if economically advantageous; and a protest periphery (approximately 8–10%) that uses separation sentiment to signal fiscal grievance without desiring independence. Moderate

Key Finding No credible economic analysis—including the APP's own fiscal plan when subjected to external review—supports the claim that an independent Alberta would be wealthier. The fiscal grievance (net contributor to Confederation) is real; the proposed remedy (separation) worsens Alberta's fiscal position by every measure examined. The economic case against separation is independent of any legal holding and survives every scenario tested.

5. Treaty Rights & Constitutional Law

5.1 The Legal Architecture

Alberta's separation faces formidable legal obstacles at multiple, independent points. Two Alberta Court of King's Bench (trial court) decisions have established that the separation proposal contravenes Treaty rights protected under s.35 of the Constitution Act, 1982, and that the Crown's duty to consult First Nations is triggered before any referendum process can advance. These are ABKB decisions under appeal, not SCC rulings; they establish a strong analytical position grounded in well-established constitutional principles but are not settled constitutional law. Strong for the legal principles; Moderate for binding precedential value pending appeal.

5.2 Sylvestre (2025 ABKB 712)

CEO of Alberta v Sylvestre, December 5, 2025. Justice Feasby ruled that the proposed independence referendum would contravene Charter rights and constitutionally protected treaty rights under s.35. The decision employed a multi-framework analysis, testing treaty-party substitution under Canadian contract law, international law, Cree legal principles, and Blackfoot historical practice. All four frameworks pointed to the same conclusion: unilateral party substitution is impermissible.8 Strong

Treaty 8 extends across 841,000+ square kilometres into BC, Saskatchewan, and the NWT. An international border cannot be drawn through the middle of a treaty territory without treaty-party consent. — Justice Feasby's reasoning in Sylvestre, 2025 ABKB 712

5.3 ACFN v Alberta (2026 ABKB 375)

Athabasca Chipewyan First Nation v Alberta (Chief Electoral Officer), May 13, 2026. Justice Leonard ruled on three independent grounds: (1) the Chief Electoral Officer erred in law regarding transition rules under the Citizens Initiative Act; (2) the CEO erred by failing to consider the Sylvestre ruling; and (3) the Crown failed its duty to consult First Nations before advancing the secession process.9 Strong

5.4 The SCC Secession Reference (1998)

The foundational legal framework. The SCC unanimously held:10

  1. No unilateral right to secession under Canadian constitutional law
  2. No unilateral right to secession under international law (Alberta, like Quebec, participates fully in democratic governance)
  3. A clear vote on a clear question triggers an obligation to negotiate in good faith—all parties must negotiate, including the seceding province
  4. Negotiations must address the interests of all parties, including minorities—subsequent jurisprudence connects this to Indigenous rights
Dual-Track Note The Secession Reference is both a prohibition and a framework. It prohibits unilateral action but creates a pathway to negotiated secession after a clear vote. The analysis presents both dimensions: the prohibition (no unilateral secession) AND the obligation to negotiate (a clear majority on a clear question creates reciprocal obligations). The analysis does not treat the Secession Reference as a permanent block; it treats it as a framework that requires negotiation, including with Treaty nations whose constitutional standing exceeds the province's.

5.5 The Haida Spectrum and the Sparrow Framework

The duty to consult sits on a spectrum established in Haida Nation v British Columbia (2004 SCC 73): from notice at the low end to full consent at the high end, calibrated to the strength of the claim and the severity of the impact.11 The analysis argues that because treaty rights are established and constitutionally protected, and because the impact of separation would be existential, the duty sits at or near the full-consent end of the spectrum. This is an analytical argument, not a legal conclusion that any court has reached in the secession context. Moderate

The Sparrow justification framework (R v Sparrow, [1990] 1 SCR 1075) establishes that s.35 rights can be infringed if the infringement meets a justification standard: compelling and substantial legislative objective, consultation with affected Aboriginal groups, minimal impairment, and priority to Aboriginal interests.12 The analysis argues that the level of infringement involved in secession—unilateral party substitution, international border through treaty territory, potential loss of constitutional protections—is so severe that meeting the Sparrow standard would be extremely difficult and without precedent. This is analytically defensible but judicially untested: no court has applied Sparrow to secession. Moderate

5.6 The Geographic Impossibility

Treaty 8 covers 841,000+ square kilometres across four current jurisdictions: northern Alberta, northeastern BC, northwestern Saskatchewan, and southwestern NWT. Strong An international border through the middle of Treaty 8 territory is not a legal theory—it is a geographic fact with unavoidable legal implications. No amount of appellate reframing eliminates the problem of bifurcating a treaty territory. This is the analysis's strongest ground and survives every scenario tested, including SCC reversal of Sylvestre.

5.7 The Clarity Act

The Clarity Act (SC 2000, c 26) codifies key elements of the Secession Reference. Section 3(2) prohibits proposing a constitutional amendment for secession until Aboriginal peoples' rights have been addressed.13 The Clarity Act is statutory reinforcement of constitutional principles—it can be amended by ordinary legislation, unlike the s.35 constitutional protections it codifies. It adds political weight but its constitutional weight derives from the principles it implements, not from its statutory status alone. Strong for current operative effect; the characterization is "statutory reinforcement" rather than "independent barrier."

5.8 Bill 14 and the October 19 Referendum

The Justice Statutes Amendment Act, 2025 (Bill 14) was introduced December 4, 2025—one day before Sylvestre. It amended the Citizens Initiative Act but was rendered ineffective by the ACFN decision. Strong Premier Smith announced the government referendum on February 19, 2026, adding Question 10 (separation) on May 21, 2026, after courts blocked the APP's citizen initiative route. The referendum is a Government of Alberta referendum, not an APP referendum. Strong

5.9 Appeal Prospects

The analysis predicts the SCC would most likely adopt a process-requirement framing—treaty rights must be substantively addressed during negotiations—rather than the structural-prohibition framing that pervades the ABKB decisions. This is a reframing, not a clean reversal: the SCC would affirm that treaty rights must be addressed while declining to hold that they constitute an absolute bar.14 Inferred

Factors favouring affirmance: well-established s.35 principles, duty-to-consult jurisprudence matured since 1998, geographic impossibility of Treaty 8 bifurcation. Factors favouring modification: novel application, trial-court methodology unprecedented, democratic legitimacy dimension of the Secession Reference.

6. Global Comparator Analysis

6.1 Category 1: Independence from Democratic States (8–10 cases)

Cases examined: United States (1776), Norway (1905), Ireland (1921), Iceland (1944), Singapore (1965), Czechoslovakia (1993), Montenegro (2006), South Sudan (2011), Bangladesh (1971). Strong

No case in Category 1 involved secession from an established, functioning democracy. Every case with a prior-authority population that did not obtain consent produced lasting harm to that population—without exception.

The Czechoslovakia Edge Case

Czechoslovakia (1993) is the most commonly cited counter-example to the "no democracy has fractured" finding. The analysis addresses it directly: it was a consensual dissolution negotiated by elites, not unilateral secession; no referendum was held and polls showed majorities in both republics opposed dissolution; the democratic experience was only 3 years (Velvet Revolution 1989); and there were no prior-authority complications. Strong Alberta has all four of those complications.15

An "established democracy" is defined as: universal suffrage, at least one peaceful transfer of power, functioning independent judiciary, protected civil liberties, and at least 10 years of continuous democratic governance. Czechoslovakia does not meet this threshold.

6.2 Category 2: Parent-State Collapse (25–30 cases)

Soviet dissolution (15 successor states), Yugoslav dissolution (7 successor states), Ottoman and Austro-Hungarian dissolution. Strong

In every case where prior-authority populations existed, those populations were either trampled (Soviet Central Asia, Kurdish populations), subject to violence (Bosnia, Chechnya, Kosovo), left in frozen conflict (Transnistria, Abkhazia), or subject to decades of delayed rights recognition (Baltic Russian speakers). There is no case in the Category 2 record where prior-authority populations emerged from state dissolution with their rights intact.16

Alberta and British Columbia present the same foundational crisis from opposite directions. Alberta has treaties that trial courts have found block unilateral separation. BC mostly lacks treaties and faces unresolved Aboriginal title that courts are now declaring over fee simple land. 7.3 The DRIPA Crisis

Premier Eby's trajectory—from championing DRIPA to amending to suspending it—demonstrates what happens when a province confronts the implications of its own foundational sovereignty gap. The BC Conservative leadership has proposed repealing DRIPA entirely. Strong

7.4 OCAP and Data Sovereignty

The question "who has authority over this data" mirrors "who has authority over this land"—and the legal architecture is the same. OCAP (Ownership, Control, Access, Possession) and CARE (Collective benefit, Authority to control, Responsibility, Ethics) principles assert Indigenous data sovereignty as an expression of broader sovereignty. This is not metaphor; it is the same prior-authority claim expressed at a different scale.20 Moderate

Cross-Provincial Convergence Three independent lines of judicial reasoning in two provinces, grounded in different legal frameworks (treaty rights in Alberta, Aboriginal title and UNDRIP in BC), arrive at the same structural conclusion: provincial authority cannot override pre-existing Indigenous legal interests. This convergence is itself a finding. Strong

8.2 Why Partnership Is the Load-Bearing Pathway

The comparator record shows that the pathway most likely to durably resolve both fiscal and identity grievances runs through Indigenous partnership, not around it. The separatist movement has positioned itself against the one group whose constitutional standing—established at the trial-court level and grounded in s.35, the Secession Reference, and duty-to-consult jurisprudence—exceeds its own. Strong

Treaty nations have sovereignty grievances with Ottawa that substantially overlap with Alberta's: resource revenue sharing, jurisdictional overreach, inadequate self-determination. A provincial-Indigenous coalition negotiating position would be more powerful than either party acting alone and would satisfy the constitutional requirement that all parties negotiate in good faith.21

8.3 The Identity vs. Economics Question

If support drops from 28% to 15% when costs are surfaced, the residual 15% is not economically motivated. What is it? The comparator evidence from Scotland, Catalonia, and the Basque Country suggests that identity grievances persist even when fiscal grievances are resolved. Constitutional evolution addresses economic grievances effectively (the Basque Concierto is proof); whether it addresses identity grievances depends on the nature of the identity claim. Moderate

Treaty Nation Plurality (ST-2) Treaty nations are not monolithic. They are diverse political communities with different leadership structures, economic situations, and views on self-determination. The analysis models three configurations: (a) unified opposition (current status—six Treaty nations have formed a joint legal front); (b) conditional support from some nations in exchange for enhanced self-governance; (c) Treaty nations pursuing independent sovereignty claims. Constitutional protections under s.35 attach to nations collectively and cannot be waived by individual leaders, so the constitutional analysis holds regardless of political unity. But the political framing—essential for the October 19 referendum context—would change significantly under configurations (b) or (c).

8.4 Comparator Pathway Evidence

The Basque Concierto Economico is the most successful example of fiscal devolution resolving a sovereignty grievance. Basque Country collects virtually all taxes and pays a negotiated sum to Madrid for common services. GDP per capita significantly exceeds the Spanish average. ETA violence ended. The grievance was better addressed through constitutional evolution than through armed struggle or secession.22 Strong

South Tyrol's 137-measure autonomy "Package" (1972) took over 20 years to implement but produced one of Europe's most successful minority-protection frameworks: GDP per capita among the highest in the EU, 54 years without reversal. Strong

Greenland's progressive self-governance (1979–2009) recognized the Greenlandic people's right to self-determination under international law, with an explicit provision for future independence. The prior-authority population drives the process. Strong

9. Political Landscape

9.1 The Decisive Window

Alberta's sovereignty crisis is entering a decisive six-month window. The October 19, 2026, referendum—including Question 10 on separation—is four months away. Two court decisions have blocked the process on treaty-rights grounds. Public support has dropped from 28% (January 2026) to 18% (June 2026), with only 8% committed regardless of consequences. Strong

9.2 Five Theatres

Theatre 1: APP-UCP Dynamic

The Alberta Prosperity Project's citizen-initiative route to a referendum was struck down by Sylvestre and ACFN. Premier Smith absorbed the separation question into her government's broader 10-question referendum, positioning herself on the Remain side with a $400B cost estimate and an August costing deadline. Smith is appealing the court decisions while simultaneously using the referendum to surface costs that undermine the separatist position—a strategy of controlled detonation. Moderate

Theatre 2: Treaty Nations' Legal and Advocacy Strategy

Six Treaty nations have formed a unified legal and public advocacy front: Athabasca Chipewyan (Treaty 8), Siksika Nation (Treaty 7), and four additional nations spanning Treaties 6, 7, and 8. Their strategy combines: legal challenge (court filings), public framing ("Alberta came after Treaty 8"), constitutional positioning (prior-authority parties, not stakeholders), and independent referendum monitoring. Strong

Theatre 3: Federal Positioning

The Carney government faces a multi-front challenge: manage the Alberta referendum, respond to BC's DRIPA crisis, maintain national unity, and pursue equalization reform. Federal engagement is necessary for all constitutional evolution pathways but cannot be assumed as always available. If a federal political crisis makes constitutional evolution impossible, the separatist argument strengthens because the only response to the fiscal grievance (reform within Confederation) becomes demonstrably unavailable. Moderate

Theatre 4: Public Opinion and Media

The polling trajectory is unfavourable for the separatist movement: 28% (January) to 18% (June), with the August costing document expected to further erode support. The "regardless of consequences" hardcore is 8%. Media coverage is increasingly focused on costs and legal obstacles rather than the fiscal grievance. Strong

Theatre 5: Cross-Provincial Convergence with BC

BC's DRIPA crisis and Aboriginal title decisions are creating a parallel political dynamic. The convergence is not metaphorical—the same foundational question (provincial authority over Indigenous land) is being litigated simultaneously in two provinces through different legal frameworks, producing reinforcing judicial findings. Strong

14. Dual-Track Analysis

The stress test (Scenario 2) identified the Sylvestre/ACFN appeal as the single most important test of the analysis's durability. The analysis presents findings under both plausible SCC outcomes.

14.1 Track A: Sylvestre/ACFN Affirmed

If the SCC affirms the ABKB holdings—treaty rights as a structural prohibition on separation—then:

  • Alberta remains in Category 6 with no historical precedent for a derivative entity successfully separating over prior-authority objections
  • The October 19 referendum's separation question (Q10) would be constitutionally blocked
  • Constitutional evolution pathways requiring Treaty nation consent become the only viable routes
  • The chain-of-title argument is judicially validated as both narrative and doctrine
  • The precedent would apply to any future secession attempt on treaty land globally

14.2 Track B: SCC Reframes to Process Requirement

If the SCC reframes treaty rights as a negotiation topic rather than a precondition—the outcome the analysis itself predicts as most likely—then:

  • Alberta shifts toward a Category 4/6 hybrid: an active democratic movement facing prior-authority claims that function as negotiation constraints
  • The referendum could proceed, but treaty rights must be substantively addressed during post-referendum negotiations
  • The geographic impossibility of Treaty 8 remains (factual, not dependent on legal holdings)
  • The economic case against separation remains (independent of legal framework)
  • The base-rate finding (zero established democracies have fractured) and capital-flight evidence remain
  • The framing shifts from "constitutionally blocked" to "constitutionally constrained by process requirements that Treaty nations have stated they will enforce"

15. Limitations & Evidence Gaps

  1. Category 6 sample size. Five to seven cases is insufficient for statistical generalization. The directional consistency of the evidence is strong, but a future counter-example cannot be ruled out. Moderate
  2. ABKB trial-court holdings. The analysis's central legal findings rest on two ABKB decisions under appeal. These are grounded in well-established constitutional principles but have not been tested at the SCC. The dual-track presentation addresses this limitation.
  3. Tombe-Kaplan methodological ancestry. Two of the three converging economic analyses share methodological roots. The Brexit extrapolation is genuinely independent, but the convergence is less robust than three fully independent analyses.
  4. Alberta-specific capital flight data. Capital flight has been documented for Quebec and Catalonia but has not yet occurred in Alberta. The prediction is an inference from comparator evidence, not a directly observed finding. Inferred
  5. Indigenous sovereignty framing. This analysis uses Western analytical frameworks. Indigenous nations' own sovereignty narratives—which frame treaties as sharing agreements rather than land surrenders, and which ground sovereignty in relationships rather than territorial control—may not be adequately captured by the 24-feature matrix. The analysis centers the Alberta question and treats Indigenous sovereignty as a constraint, even as it acknowledges that Indigenous sovereignty is the prior claim. This framing is a structural feature, not a fixable flaw.
  6. Missing voices. The analysis centers formal political leadership recognized within the Indian Act governance system. Perspectives of Indigenous women, Two-Spirit people, and urban Indigenous populations are not separately represented. This limitation applies to both the Alberta analysis and the global comparator cases.
  7. Federal engagement assumed. All seven constitutional evolution pathways require federal engagement to varying degrees. The analysis does not adequately address what happens when the federal partner is dysfunctional. The Basque Country waited decades for the right political moment (post-Franco transition); institutional preparation during periods of federal dysfunction is the relevant comparator response.
  8. "Proves too much" unresolved. The chain-of-title argument's strongest critique—that it delegitimizes all provincial authority—has not been resolved by any court. The analysis offers three layers of response, all its own construction. A court might accept, reject, or reformulate the distinction.
  9. Sparrow framework application to secession. No court has applied the Sparrow justification framework to secession. The analysis argues the infringement would be so severe as to be unjustifiable, but this is the analysis's own construction. Moderate
  10. ICJ Kosovo advisory opinion. The analysis does not fully engage with the ICJ's advisory opinion (2010) holding that "international law contains no prohibition on declarations of independence." While this does not change the domestic constitutional analysis (Alberta has full democratic participation, unlike Kosovo), the international law treatment is incomplete.
  11. Treaty nation plurality. The analysis models three Treaty nation configurations but the current analysis primarily assumes unified opposition. Political dynamics among and within Treaty 6, 7, and 8 nations are not independently verified.
  12. APP economic analysis. No professionally audited version of the APP's fiscal plan has been published. If one were produced, it would need assessment on its merits. The analysis critiques the published plan, not a hypothetical improved version.
