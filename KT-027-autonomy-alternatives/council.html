What Alberta Should Do Instead of Separation

A Sequenced Autonomy Roadmap

The separatist framing offers two choices: accept the status quo or leave. Both are wrong. Between those poles sit institutional mechanisms that deliver more fiscal autonomy than separation, without the $30–39B/year GDP shock. The Basque Country collects all its own taxes, remits a negotiated €1.5B/year to Madrid, and enjoys 29.8% higher fiscal capacity than other Spanish regions. Quebec runs its own revenue agency, selects its own immigrants, and maintains 32 international offices. Alberta does none of this. The question is not whether Alberta has grievances. It does. The question is whether separation is the only instrument that addresses them.

The Roadmap

Now — Existing Authority
  1. Entrench the Heritage Fund in the constitution (Section 45 — no federal consent needed)
  2. Accelerate Heritage Fund deposits — $93 WTI is the window; delay is permanent loss
  3. Expand AAIP immigration nominees from 6,403 to 15,000–25,000
  4. Scale Invest Alberta from 18 to 25–28 international offices
Existing provincial jurisdiction under Sections 45, 92, 95
2–5 Years — Federal Agreement
  1. Alberta Revenue Agency ($500–750M/yr) — prerequisite for fiscal autonomy
  2. Comprehensive immigration accord modelled on the 1991 Canada-Quebec Accord
  3. Tax point transfer — 16.5% federal abatement equivalent
Federal-provincial negotiation; Quebec precedent established
25–50 Years — Constitutional Moment
  1. Basque-style Concierto Económico — Alberta collects all taxes, remits a negotiated amount to Ottawa
Requires constitutional amendment (Section 36/equalization); delivers more fiscal autonomy than separation

The Basque precedent changes the calculus. The Concierto Económico delivers what separatists say only independence can: full tax collection authority, negotiated fiscal transfers, and institutional distinctiveness. The Basque Country has had this since 1878. Nobody in Alberta's sovereignty debate is discussing it.

What This Does Not Address

The identity grievance persists. Ipsos polling shows 28% support for separation dropping to 15–16% when costs are surfaced — but 8% support separation "regardless of consequences." That cohort is identity-driven, not fiscal. A revenue agency does not make someone feel less alienated by Ottawa. Some alternatives address identity (international presence, Sovereignty Act symbolism, immigration control). Most address fiscal mechanics only.

These are slow paths. Revenue agency: 3–5 years. Heritage Fund: 25 years to $250B. Basque-style arrangement: constitutional timeline unknown. If you are angry now, these timelines are frustrating. That is an honest assessment, not an argument against them.

Treaty Nations as Design Partners

Treaties 6, 7, and 8 predate Alberta by decades. Treaty Nations are prior-authority parties, not stakeholders to be consulted after the fact. Every alternative in this roadmap has been assessed for Treaty interaction. Heritage Fund entrenchment must include Indigenous benefit-sharing provisions. A revenue agency must coordinate with emerging Indigenous fiscal institutions. The May 13, 2026, Court of King's Bench ruling makes Treaty consultation legally mandatory for any initiative affecting Treaty rights.

Alberta has legitimate fiscal grievances and real institutional options that no provincial government has seriously pursued. This roadmap sequences eight alternatives by constitutional pathway, costs each one, and identifies the Basque Concierto Económico as the long-horizon option that delivers more fiscal autonomy than separation — at a fraction of the cost and none of the existential risk. The first four steps require no permission from Ottawa. Alberta can start tomorrow.
