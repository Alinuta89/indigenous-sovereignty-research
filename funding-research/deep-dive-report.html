Chapter 1
The Scale No One Agrees On
How much does Canada spend on Indigenous services? It depends entirely on which lens you use.
TierWhat's IncludedAnnual EstimateSource

Standard policy analysis uses Tier 1 — proactive disclosure of federal grants above $25,000. This captures roughly half of actual federal Indigenous spending. The $20 billion gap between Tier 1 and Tier 2 = internal operations (~8,600 ISC staff), capital expenditures, statutory obligations, below-threshold transfers, and programs embedded in 19 departments beyond ISC and CIRNAC.

Specific claims settlements ($11.5B over 11 years) are entirely absent from proactive disclosure because they are statutory legal obligations, not discretionary grants. Provincial Indigenous ministry spending ($1.5B/yr across 13 jurisdictions) adds another layer invisible in federal data. And at the band level, FNFTA audited financial statements reveal that on-reserve members receive 3.7x what proactive disclosure suggests — because gaming revenue, resource IBAs, and own-source business income are invisible at every federal tier.

The question "how much does Canada spend on Indigenous services" has no single answer. Every number is defensible; the difference is scope. This report uses all five tiers.

Chapter 2
Following the Federal Money
$40-44 billion per year across 21 departments — quadrupled in a decade.
+405%
real growth in federal Indigenous spending since 2015-16
$10B (2015-16) to $44B (2024-25) in 2024 CAD · structural, not COVID

In 2015-16, total federal Indigenous spending was $10 billion. By 2024-25, it had reached approximately $44 billion across 21 departments. Three structural shifts drove this: the 2016 FNCFS Tribunal ruling (child welfare reform), UNDRIP implementation commitments, and expanded self-government settlements under CIRNAC.

ISC and CIRNAC account for ~93% of total spending, but the remaining 7% ($3.1B) is distributed across Finance Canada, ESDC, PHAC, Justice, DFO, NRCan, Canadian Heritage, CMHC, and Public Safety — departments not traditionally associated with Indigenous services but whose Indigenous-tagged programming has grown 13x in real terms since 2015.

The system is extraordinarily concentrated. The First Nations Health Authority (BC) — a single tripartite agreement covering 204 First Nations — absorbs 37.8% of all disclosed federal transfers ($8.30B in 2024 CAD). The top 10 recipients take 49.4%. Excluding FNHA, BC's share drops from 47% to ~9%.

Chapter 3
The Settlement Layer
$11.5 billion in specific claims — entirely invisible in standard funding analysis.

Between 2013 and 2024, the Government of Canada settled 330 specific claims for treaty violations and unlawful land takings, paying $11.5 billion in compensation. These payments are statutory legal obligations — not discretionary grants — and appear nowhere in proactive disclosure data. The $2.5 billion paid in 2023-24 alone exceeds Alberta's entire five-year provincial grant total.

Settlement values have accelerated dramatically: average annual payout was $144M before 2017, rising to $1.57B/yr since 2017 — an 11x increase. The largest individual settlements:

Siksika Nation's $1.3 billion is the largest single-nation specific claims settlement in Canadian history. Six additional Alberta First Nations have confirmed settlements in 2023-25 (Frog Lake, Kehewin Cree, Alexis Nakota Sioux, Enoch Cree, Heart Lake, Sturgeon Lake). With 23 Alberta claims pending at the Specific Claims Tribunal — second only to BC's 45 — significant future settlement flow is expected.

Chapter 4
What Provinces Invest
$1.5 billion across 13 jurisdictions — a 68x per-capita spread.

Provincial and territorial Indigenous affairs spending totals approximately $1.5 billion per year in dedicated ministry budgets. This is an undercount — Indigenous service spending is dispersed across Health, Education, Justice, and Children's Services in every province. But the dedicated ministry number reveals the priority gap: Yukon invests $3,212 per Indigenous person while Newfoundland invests $47.

Gaming revenue is a parallel channel invisible in ministry budgets. Saskatchewan's SIGA payments ($120.6M) exceed its ministry budget. Alberta's FNDF is the largest but least transparent — no per-First Nation disclosure. BC's 7% BCLC revenue-sharing model (through 2045) is the most structured: 50% equal share, 40% by population, 10% remote communities.

What Bands Actually Receive
FNFTA ground truth: 3.7x more than proactive disclosure shows.

Proactive disclosure shows what the government sends. FNFTA audited financial statements show what bands receive. The difference is 3.7x — because gaming revenue, resource Impact Benefit Agreements, provincial transfers, and own-source business income are all invisible in federal disclosure.

Chapter 7
The Funding Network
42,095 edges, 41 communities, and a structural fragility crisis.
84%
of funded organizations depend on a single funder
746 of 887 organizations · HHI > 0.6 · one policy change could cascade

We built a network graph of 42,095 funding relationships connecting 922 Indigenous organizations to 22 government funders. The network reveals a fundamental structural split: the provincial government provides breadth (Alberta Advanced Education alone funds 386 organizations) while the federal government provides depth (ISC moves $17 billion through 193 organizations).

Most organizations appear in the network because of a provincial grant, but their operational viability depends on federal transfers. 84% have a Herfindahl-Hirschman Index above 0.6 — meaning a single funder dominates their revenue. This is not theoretical risk: when Alberta briefly eliminated the Minister of Indigenous Relations portfolio in 2019, funding continuity was reportedly affected. With half of provincial Indigenous funding flowing through that one ministry, the single point of failure is real.

Louvain community detection identified 41 distinct funding clusters. The largest (685 orgs) is dominated by Treaty 6 charities receiving GOA provincial grants. Cold Lake First Nations, Gift Lake, and Buffalo Lake Metis Settlement are the most-connected nodes — each with 15 unique funders.

Alberta receives $9,389 per Indigenous person per year in federal funding — the lowest of any province. The national provincial median is approximately $13,000. The gap of roughly $3,600 per person, applied to 284,470 people, implies approximately $1.02 billion per year in systematic federal under-investment relative to the provincial median.

Five Recommendations
Evidence-based, costed, grounded in the data.
1
Urban Indigenous Funding Stream
Dedicated federal + provincial funding for housing, mental health, food security, and transition services in Calgary, Edmonton, Red Deer, and Lethbridge. 110,000 urban Indigenous people cannot be served by a system designed for reserves.
$50-75M / year
2
Treaty 8 Service Infrastructure
Mobile mental health teams, food security hubs, addictions treatment feasibility study, joint ISC-GOA emergency response protocol. Address the most acute deserts in Canada's most geographically challenging service area.

Cross-Ministry Investment Baselines
Require Health, Mental Health & Addiction, Children's Services, Justice, and Education to set Indigenous investment targets proportional to Indigenous client populations. Mental Health and Addiction alone needs to move from $3M/yr to $25-30M/yr.
Policy change + $25-30M incremental

Metis Structural Parity
Federal advocacy for ISC-equivalent Metis service delivery fund. Provincial increase to MNA operating grant. Dedicated Metis Settlements capital fund. Section 35 guarantees rights the funding architecture has never delivered.

Methodology & Data Sources12 tickets · 2 sprints · 16 agents · all official GoC sources

Official data sources: CRA T3010 charity filings, federal proactive disclosure of grants & contributions, GC InfoBase / Main Estimates, CIRNAC Specific Claims disclosures, FNFTA band financial statements, GOA grant payments disclosure, StatCan Census 2021 table 98-10-0266-01, and published provincial budget/gaming sources. AGLC and AHS dollar ranges are treated as excluded estimates unless source tables are published.

Pipeline: DuckDB (sovereign stack, DENVR Calgary), Python, NetworkX (graph analysis). Entity resolution via Jaro-Winkler similarity with auditable decision logs. All amounts CPI-adjusted to 2024 CAD (StatCan Table 18-10-0005-01). ETL-hardened pattern with 7 assertion rules on every download.

Coverage: 922 Alberta Indigenous organizations (100% known-list coverage). 35 of 48 Alberta band financials. 330 specific claims settlements. 21 federal departments. 13 provincial/territorial jurisdictions. 42,095 funding network edges. Census 2021 exact denominators (1,807,250 national match).

Key limitations: Single-year T3010 (2023). FNFTA revenue breakdown requires PDF extraction (totals only in dataset). AGLC gaming per-FN amounts not disclosed. 13 AB bands missing from FNFTA. Service domain tags ~26% populated — deserts are likely conservative. Urban org count is a significant undercount.

OCAP compliance: This research analyzes publicly disclosed government and charity data. It does not use First Nations community data. Framing throughout: government accountability for under-investment, not surveillance of Indigenous organizations.
