Step 3 of 7

Now find BC

$35,237
5.7x Alberta’s rate. Similar population.

BC: 290,210 Indigenous people. Alberta: 284,470. Nearly identical populations. BC gets 5.7 times the per-capita funding. The difference: BC has the First Nations Health Authority ($8.2B/10yr). Alberta has nothing equivalent.

Step 4 of 7

The prairie gap

Saskatchewan: $8,910. Manitoba: $11,219. Both higher than Alberta. Alberta has the largest Indigenous population of any prairie province and the lowest per-capita funding.

Step 5 of 7

What the territories show

Nunavut: $23,243. PEI: $23,036. Yukon: $12,506. When the federal government funds directly without a province in between, per-capita rates are 2-4x higher. The provincial layer is the bottleneck.

Step 6 of 7

If Alberta got the national average

$3.4B
additional funding per year

The national average is $12,200 per Indigenous person. Alberta gets $6,206. The gap: $6,000 per person × 284,470 people = $1.7B annually. At BC rates it would be $8.3B more.

Step 7 of 7

The shared grievance

Alberta sends $14.5B/year more to Ottawa than it gets back. Treaty Nations receive among the lowest per-capita Indigenous funding in the country. Same complaint. Same cause. Different victims of the same architecture. The coalition argument starts here.
