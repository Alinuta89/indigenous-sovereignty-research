Why the federal number is misleading
The federal government spends 4.8x more per Indigenous person than per general Albertan in direct transfers. But this is not generosity — federal Indigenous funding covers what the province covers for everyone else: healthcare, education, child welfare, housing. On-reserve, the federal government IS the provincial government. The real comparison is the combined picture: when you add both levels together, Indigenous Albertans receive 52 cents for every dollar spent on the general population. The province spends $17,568 per person on all Albertans through its budget, but only $848 per Indigenous person in Indigenous-specific programming.

Understanding the domain-level gaps
These figures compare total Alberta ministry budgets (serving all Albertans) with Indigenous-specific provincial grant payments only. They are floor estimates — federal ISC spending adds to Indigenous health, education, and child welfare but is not split by domain in this analysis. Even so, the gaps are stark: the province allocates $6,282 per person for healthcare but only $3.64 per Indigenous person in Indigenous-specific health grants. Mental health is the most extreme outlier at 3 cents on the dollar.

The Starkest Gap: Mental Health
For every dollar Alberta spends on mental health and addiction services for the general population, Indigenous Albertans receive 3 cents in Indigenous-specific provincial grants. One organization serves a population of 284,470 people. The province’s Mental Health and Addiction ministry budget is $1.06 billion — but Indigenous-specific mental health grants total approximately $2.1 million per year.

Methodology and Caveats
swap_horiz
Federal Substitution. Federal Indigenous funding covers what provinces cover for everyone else. The 4.8x federal multiplier is not generosity — it is substitution. Comparing raw federal per-capita figures without this context overstates federal effort and understates provincial neglect.
visibility_off
AGLC Gaming Revenue. AGLC/FNDF gaming is an excluded transparency gap. The $200–350M/yr range is an unverified planning estimate, not a reproduced source value, and it appears in neither column of this comparison.
groups
Census Denominators. Population figures use Statistics Canada Census 2021 (Aboriginal identity, single responses). Alberta Indigenous population = 284,470. These are PRELIMINARY and likely undercounted by 10–15% due to incomplete reserve enumeration. Final figures will slightly reduce per-capita estimates.
gavel
CHRT 2016 Ruling. The Canadian Human Rights Tribunal (2016 CHRT 2) found that Canada’s underfunding of First Nations child welfare on-reserve was discriminatory. Compliance is ongoing. This analysis provides the broader Alberta context for that finding.
home
On-Reserve vs Off-Reserve. Off-reserve Indigenous people access provincial services alongside all other Albertans. On-reserve communities rely on federal funding intended to match provincial service levels — but as this analysis shows, the match falls short.
arrow_downward
Floor Estimates. Domain-level Indigenous spending uses provincial grant disclosure only. Federal ISC allocations to Alberta are not split by service domain in this analysis. The domain ratios are therefore floor estimates — actual Indigenous spending per domain is higher but still far below general-population levels.
