The National Picture
Far more money flows than any single lens reveals

The federal government's full Indigenous commitment is $40-44 billion per year across 21 departments — not the $19-22B visible in proactive disclosure. The gap: internal operations, salaries, capital, below-threshold transfers, and programs in Finance (26.5% of indigenous-priority spend), ESDC (18.4%), PHAC, Justice, and 17 other departments. In 2015-16, the figure was $10 billion. In a decade, it has quadrupled.

On top of this, $11.5 billion in specific claims settlements flowed to First Nations over the past 11 years (330 claims, 2013-2024) — entirely invisible in standard grant disclosure because they are statutory legal obligations, not discretionary spending. Siksika Nation (Alberta) received $1.3 billion — the largest single-nation settlement in Canadian history.

Yet the system remains extraordinarily concentrated. The First Nations Health Authority (BC) absorbs 37.8% of all disclosed federal transfers. The top 10 recipients take 49.4%. And the standard lens — proactive disclosure — captures only half of actual federal Indigenous spending.

The Alberta Gap
Last among all provinces in per-capita federal Indigenous funding

$9,389 per person vs. ~$13,000 national median · 284,470 Indigenous people

But here is the twist: audited band financial statements (FNFTA) tell a different story. Alberta First Nations actually receive $34,774 per on-reserve member — 3.7x the proactive disclosure figure. The gap = gaming revenue (Enoch's River Cree Casino), resource IBAs (Fort McKay at $244,000/capita from oil sands agreements), provincial transfers, and own-source business revenue — all invisible in federal disclosure.

The provincial government adds $241M/yr in disclosed grants — a 21:1 federal-to-provincial ratio. Mental Health and Addiction contributes $3 million per year for 284,000 people. AGLC/FNDF gaming revenue is a major excluded transparency gap: the $200-350M/yr range is an unverified planning estimate, not an input to headline arithmetic.

The visibility problem
Standard policy analysis uses Tier 1 — proactive disclosure of grants above $25K. This captures roughly half of actual federal Indigenous spending. Specific claims settlements ($11.5B over 11 years) are entirely absent. And band-level revenue from gaming, resource agreements, and own-source business is invisible at every federal tier. The "how much does Canada spend on Indigenous services" question has no single answer — it depends entirely on which lens you use.

Provincial Landscape
$1.5B across 13 jurisdictions — wildly uneven investment

Provincial Indigenous affairs spending totals approximately $1.5 billion per year across all 13 jurisdictions — a fraction of the federal $40-44B. But provincial investment varies by 68x per capita: Yukon spends $3,212 per Indigenous person vs. Newfoundland at $47. Quebec's $405.8M Secrétariat aux affaires autochtones is the largest dedicated provincial ministry in Canada.

Network Analysis
A single policy change could destabilize 84% of the funded infrastructure

We built a network graph of 42,095 funding relationships connecting 922 Indigenous organizations to their funders. The network reveals a fundamental structural split:

The provincial government provides breadth — many small grants to many organizations. Alberta Advanced Education alone funds 386 Indigenous organizations. But the federal government provides depth — ISC moves $17 billion through 193 organizations.

Most organizations appear in the network because of a provincial grant, but their operational viability depends on federal transfers. 746 of 887 funded organizations (84%) have a Herfindahl-Hirschman Index above 0.6 — meaning a single funder dominates their revenue. A budget cut, a program cancellation, or an audit freeze could cascade across the entire organizational landscape.

Methodology & Data Sources 100% open data · reproducible · OCAP-compliant

Data sources: CRA T3010 charity filings, federal proactive disclosure of grants & contributions, GC InfoBase / Main Estimates, CIRNAC Specific Claims disclosures, FNFTA band financial statements, GOA grant payments disclosure, and StatCan Census 2021 table 98-10-0266-01. AGLC and AHS dollar ranges are excluded estimates until source tables are published.

Pipeline: DuckDB (sovereign stack, DENVR Calgary), Python, NetworkX (graph analysis). Entity resolution via Jaro-Winkler similarity with auditable decision logs. All amounts CPI-adjusted to 2024 CAD (StatCan Table 18-10-0005-01). ETL-hardened pattern: every download validated, every load asserted against known totals.

Coverage: 11 research tickets across 2 sprints. 21 federal departments mapped. 922 Alberta Indigenous orgs. 35 of 48 Alberta band financials captured. 330 specific claims settlements. 42,095 funding network edges.

Key limitations: Single-year T3010 snapshot (2023). FNFTA revenue breakdown (federal/provincial/own-source) requires PDF extraction from audited statements — totals only in current dataset. AGLC gaming data gap ($200-350M/yr unverified estimate, excluded from calculations). 13 of 48 AB bands missing from FNFTA. Service domain tags ~26% populated.

OCAP compliance: This research analyzes publicly disclosed government and charity data. It does not use First Nations community data. Framing throughout: government accountability for under-investment, not surveillance of Indigenous organizations.
