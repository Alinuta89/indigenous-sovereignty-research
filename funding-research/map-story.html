Step 1 of 8

Alberta’s Indigenous service infrastructure

809
organizations mapped across the province

Each dot is an organization serving Indigenous communities. Teal = diversified funding. This is every formal service provider T7 found.

Step 2 of 8

Now show the risk

652
organizations depend on a single funder (red dots)

84% of the map turns red. One budget decision and these organizations lose everything. This is not organizational failure — it is how the funding was designed.

Step 3 of 8

Treaty 6 territory

435
organizations in Treaty 6 (central Alberta)

The densest cluster. Maskwacis, Edmonton, Saddle Lake, Lac La Biche. Most of Alberta’s Indigenous service infrastructure concentrates here. But density doesn’t mean adequacy.

Step 4 of 8

Treaty 7 territory

143
organizations in Treaty 7 (southern Alberta)

Siksika, Standoff (Kainai/Blood Tribe), Morley (Stoney Nakoda), Brocket (Piikani). The Blackfoot Confederacy and allies. Fewer organizations, spread across southern Alberta.

Step 5 of 8

Treaty 8 territory

93
organizations in Treaty 8 (northern Alberta)

The largest treaty territory in Alberta — 841,000+ km². Many communities accessible only by air or ice road. 93 organizations for a territory larger than most European countries. Fort Chipewyan, Wabasca, High Level, Chateh. The emptiness on the map IS the finding.

Step 6 of 8

The urban concentration

Zoom into Edmonton and Calgary. Most organizations cluster here — but 60% of Indigenous Albertans live off-reserve in these cities, accessing mainstream services that are not culturally safe. The map shows formal Indigenous orgs. It does not show the 284,470 people they’re supposed to serve.

Step 7 of 8

The service deserts

1 mental health organization. 4 food security. 5 emergency services. For the entire province. You cannot see what is not there — but look at northern Alberta. Look at the spaces between dots. Those spaces are communities without formal mental health, food security, or emergency infrastructure.

Step 8 of 8

Treaties predate Alberta

Treaty 6: 1876. Treaty 7: 1877. Treaty 8: 1899. Alberta: 1905. The service deserts exist on treaty territory. The absence of services is not a program gap — it is a failure to meet treaty obligations the province inherited but did not create.
