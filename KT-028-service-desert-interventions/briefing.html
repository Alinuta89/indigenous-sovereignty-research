Service Desert Interventions

Executive Briefing • Tier 2 • June 2026
Treaties 6, 7, and 8 predate Alberta (1905). Treaty Nations are prior-authority parties. Service deserts are treaty violations, not program gaps. All interventions are proposals for Nation consideration.

The Problem

The Indigenous-Funding-Research T7 gap analysis mapped 922 Indigenous-serving organizations across Alberta and found catastrophic absences: 1 mental health organization, 4 food security organizations, and 5 emergency service organizations for 284,470 Indigenous Albertans across 600,000+ km² of treaty territory.

84% of organizations depend on a single funder. One funding decision can eliminate an entire service category. The estimated annual funding gap is $1.02B — a floor using government definitions of adequacy, not community definitions.

These deserts are not accidents. They are the downstream consequence of a colonial funding architecture (KT-026) that fragments $2.5–3B in Crown-to-Nation flows across dozens of non-communicating programs.

What We Designed

Six research streams designed intervention blueprints for each service desert. Each specifies service models, funding architecture that survives single-funder fragility, workforce requirements, and governance structures.

A. Mental Health

Hub-and-spoke network with treaty-based regional governance. Culturally grounded models (Two-Eyed Seeing, land-based, Elder-integrated). $50–90M/yr. 60–105 workers.

B. Food & Emergency

Community food sovereignty infrastructure. Emergency first responder training + community paramedic stations. Climate adaptation. $50–120M capital + $30–60M/yr.

C. Housing

Reserve mold remediation + new construction. Indigenous-governed Housing First (urban). Nation-led housing authorities. $460M–1.5B capital + $30–75M/yr.

D. Addictions

Treatment capacity expansion beyond Poundmaker’s Lodge. Land-based treatment. Harm reduction. Aftercare. $45–90M capital + $40–80M/yr.

E. Coordination

Multi-service community health centres. OCAP-compliant data systems. Nation-level coordination authority. Pooled funding mechanism. $51–82M/yr.

F. Workforce

Community Health Workers as front line (6–12 month pipeline). Post-secondary pipeline (4–10 years). Credentialing for Indigenous knowledge holders. $18–46M/yr.

2. Community engagement is absent. This research produces proposals, not plans. No intervention is valid without Nation-level authorization.

4. Provincial revenue is commodity-dependent. The 15–30% provincial contribution is vulnerable to oil price downturns.

Comparator Context

Alberta has no equivalent to BC’s First Nations Health Authority ($8.2B/10yr, 200+ communities), New Zealand’s Whānau Ora (outcomes-based commissioning, NZ$180M/4yr), or Australia’s NACCHO network (148 community-controlled health organizations, 3.6M episodes of care/year). These models demonstrate that Indigenous-governed health systems work at scale.
