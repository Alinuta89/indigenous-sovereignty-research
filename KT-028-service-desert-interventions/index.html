This research concerns Treaties 6 (1876), 7 (1877), and 8 (1899) — agreements between the Crown and Blackfoot Confederacy, Tsuut’ina Nation, Stoney Nakoda, Cree Nations, Dene, and Chipewyan Nations. These treaties predate Alberta (1905). Treaty Nations are prior-authority parties with sovereign claims over the well-being of their peoples. All intervention designs are proposals for Nation consideration, not prescriptions.

What this is

The Indigenous-Funding-Research T7 gap analysis mapped 922 Indigenous-serving organizations across Alberta and found catastrophic service deserts: 1 mental health organization, 4 food security organizations, and 5 emergency service organizations for 284,470 Indigenous Albertans. 84% of organizations depend on a single funder. The estimated annual funding gap is $1.02B — a government-definition floor.

This ticket designs the interventions: what service models, what funding architecture, what workforce, what coordination, what governance. Six research streams cover mental health, food security and emergency services, housing and homelessness, addictions and harm reduction, cross-domain coordination, and workforce development.

All intervention designs are proposals for community consideration, not recommendations for implementation. No intervention is valid without Nation-level engagement and authorization.
