Service Desert Intervention Design

Working Document for Policy Designers & Indigenous Health Planners • Tier 1
Treaty Nations (6, 7, 8) are prior-authority parties. All intervention models are proposals for Nation consideration. All cost estimates are assumption-grade. Community-level engagement and costing are prerequisites for implementation.

How to Use This Document

Each stream presents the scale of the gap, what currently exists, proposed intervention models with cost ranges, funding architecture, and workforce requirements. Evidence tagged by tradition: Western Indigenous Mixed and by strength: STRONG MODERATE INFERRED.

Culturally Grounded Models

  • Two-Eyed Seeing Mixed MODERATE — integrates Indigenous and Western knowledge. Elder Albert Marshall, 2004.
  • Land-based healing Indigenous MODERATE — time on the land as therapeutic intervention.
  • Elder-integrated counselling Indigenous — Elders as therapeutic partners with governance authority.
  • Cultural continuity Indigenous — communities with higher cultural continuity have lower suicide rates (Chandler & Lalonde, 2008).

Funding

Federal (35–40%) + Provincial (20–25%) + Nation own-source (15–20%) + other (10–20%). No source above 40%.

Stream D: Addictions & Harm Reduction

8.4x opioid death rate for First Nations (2022, AFNIGC) STRONG. Poundmaker’s Lodge: 100 beds, 68% from single funder. Intergenerational trauma is causal architecture, not background.

Two-Eyed Seeing Integration

Three models: parallel systems, integrated (e.g. Poundmaker’s), or Two-Eyed Seeing (equal authority). The Nation decides. Mixed

Proposed Interventions

Treatment expansion (2–3 centres, 50–75 beds each): $30–60M capital, $15–30M/yr. Community programs (10–15 communities): $8–15M/yr. Land-based camps: $3–6M/yr. Harm reduction: $5.5–11M/yr. Aftercare: $15–30M capital, $6–12M/yr.

Stream E: Cross-Domain Coordination

Surveillance Risk

Cross-domain data sharing tracks vulnerable people across services. OCAP requires Nation-controlled data governance as prerequisite to coordination, not afterthought.

Proposed Framework

  • Community level: Multi-service health centres or mobile teams
  • Tribal Council level: Shared admin, coordinated delivery, pooled funding
  • Treaty level: Policy coordination, data governance
  • Provincial level: Government-to-government

Pooled funding mechanism: One admin interface for orgs, multiple funders pool into single stream. Solves admin overload of blended funding.

Cross-Cutting Requirements
  • No single funder above 40% — every intervention uses blended funding
  • Community authority — every design is a proposal; Nation governance required
  • OCAP first — data governance before coordination systems
  • Support community care — formal services must not displace kinship, ceremony, mutual aid
  • Metis inclusion decision — each intervention must specify with non-ISC funding if included
