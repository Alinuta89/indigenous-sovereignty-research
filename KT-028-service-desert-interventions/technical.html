Service Desert Intervention Design

Capacity-Building Blueprints for Indigenous Alberta
Tier 0: Full Research Record • June 2026
Treaty acknowledgment. This research concerns Treaties 6 (1876), 7 (1877), and 8 (1899) — agreements between the Crown and Blackfoot Confederacy (Siksika, Piikani, Kainai), Tsuut’ina Nation, Stoney Nakoda, Cree Nations, Dene, and Chipewyan Nations. These treaties predate Alberta (1905). The service deserts documented here exist on treaty territory. Treaty Nations are prior-authority parties. All intervention designs are proposals for Nation consideration, not prescriptions.

1. Methodology and Limitations

This analysis synthesizes the Indigenous-Funding-Research T7 gap analysis, fiscal architecture findings, published health data from FNIHB, AHS, CIHI, and Statistics Canada, and program documentation from Indigenous organizations. Six parallel research streams, three adversarial red team perspectives, nine stress test scenarios, and two citation verification passes.

The single most important limitation:

This analysis was produced without community engagement. No First Nation, Tribal Council, or Metis governance body was consulted. Every model proposed is conditional on Nation-level engagement and authorization. This document produces raw material for community decision-making, not implementation plans.

Evidence tagged by tradition (Western clinical, Indigenous knowledge, mixed) and strength (STRONG, MODERATE, INFERRED). All cost estimates are assumption-grade.1

2. The T7 Evidence Base

922 Indigenous-serving organizations mapped across Alberta. Critical service deserts:2

84% of organizations depend on a single funder.3 Annual gap: $1.02B (government-definition floor; community-defined need may be 2–3x).4

The 922-organization map captures formal infrastructure only

It does not capture kinship care, ceremony-based healing, mutual aid, Elder support, or land-based wellness. The absence of formal organizations does not equal absence of care.5

Population

284,470 Indigenous Albertans (6.8%, Census 2021). First Nations: 145,640 (51.2%). Metis: 127,475 (44.8%). ~60% off-reserve.6 Metis structurally excluded from ISC/FNIHB.7

3. Stream A: Mental Health

One mental health organization. Gap ratio: entire infrastructure must be built.8

Culturally grounded evidence

Two-Eyed Seeing (Etuaptmumk). Elder Albert D. Marshall, 2004. Improved engagement, reduced dropout.9 Land-based healing. Robust in Indigenous knowledge; Western evidence emerging.10 Cultural continuity. Higher continuity = lower suicide (Chandler & Lalonde, 2008).11

Proposed network

Portfolio: 4 urban hubs + treaty-based coordination + telehealth + CHWs. $50–90M/yr. 60–105 workers. Cultural safety governed by Nation.12

Funding

Federal (35–40%) + Provincial (20–25%) + Nation (15–20%) + other (10–20%). No source above 40%.13

4. Stream B: Food Security & Emergency

4 food security + 5 emergency orgs. Food sovereignty (Nation-defined, treaty-protected) is the framework.14 Emergency response to fly-in: 2–6 hours. Fire protection budget: $20.9M/3yr nationally (equipment, not capacity).15 Climate: wildfire season +30 days since 1970s.16

Combined: $50–120M capital + $30–60M/yr.

5. Stream C: Housing & Homelessness

Reserve backlog: 3,000–6,000 units. 30–40% mold-affected. Edmonton: 50.3% Indigenous (2024 PiT); Calgary: 25%.17 74% of youth in care are Indigenous — child welfare is the pipeline.18 Jurisdictional gap: federal on-reserve, provincial off-reserve. Jordan’s Principle does not cover housing.19 Housing First with Indigenous adaptations.20

Combined: $460M–1.5B capital (5yr) + $30–75M/yr.

6. Stream D: Addictions & Harm Reduction

8.4x opioid death rate (First Nations, 2022, AFNIGC).21 24% of deaths despite 3.4% of population. Metis data not tracked.22 Poundmaker’s Lodge: 100 beds, 68% single-funder, Iskwew turned women away 2018.23 Intergenerational trauma is causal architecture.24 Thunderbird IWF: Hope, Belonging, Meaning, Purpose.25

Combined: $45–90M capital + $40–80M/yr.

7. Stream E: Cross-Domain Coordination

ISC data systems not interconnected. 84% single-funder fragments coordination. Recovery Alberta (Sept 2024) added jurisdictional seam.26

Surveillance risk

Cross-domain data sharing creates infrastructure tracking vulnerable people. OCAP requires Nation-controlled data governance as prerequisite.27

Pooled funding

Blended funding creates admin overload for small orgs. Proposed: pooled mechanism with one admin interface, multiple funders behind it.28

Comparators

FNHA (BC): $8.2B/10yr, three-body governance.29 Whānau Ora (NZ): outcomes commissioning, NZ$180M/4yr.30 NACCHO (Australia): 148 ACCHOs, 3.6M episodes/yr.31 SANKS (Norway): Indigenous MH within universal system.32

8. Stream F: Workforce

Combined need: 340–665 workers. Pipeline: 50–150 grads/yr, 50–70% retention. Timeline: best 7–10yr, likely 10–15yr.33 CHWs as front line (short-term). Credentialing for knowledge holders: parallel, team-based, or Nation-level regulation.34 Community members are the primary workforce; clinicians are specialized support.35 Remote total compensation: $100–250K (not salary alone).36

10. Red Team Findings

Three perspectives, 19 findings total.38

Indigenous Health Policy Analyst (7 findings)

$1.02B undercount insufficiently stressed. Community engagement needs stronger weighting. Western evidence bias persists in structural detail. "1 org" risks instrumentalization. Metis shallow. Recovery Alberta unassessed. Jordan’s Principle cost-shifting underanalyzed.

Provincial Fiscal Analyst (6 findings)

All costs are assumptions. Revenue volatility unaddressed. Remote premium not disaggregated. Workforce costs understated. Timeline optimistic. Urban-remote balance unclear.

Community Organizer (6 findings)

922-org map is outsider’s view. Capacity building reads as import. Blended funding creates admin overload. Designs institutional, not relational. Mutual aid invisible. Written for funders, not communities.

12. Limitations

  1. Community engagement absence. The single most important limitation.
  2. Cost estimates are assumptions. No costed interventions.
  3. T7 repo not directly verified. Service desert counts accepted from documentation.
  4. Western evidence bias. Structural detail favors Western models.
  5. Metis analysis shallow. Requires separate MNA/MSGC engagement.
  6. Workforce data absent. No public count of Indigenous health workers.
  7. Remote costs uncertain. 3–5x premium is literature-based.
  8. Recovery Alberta unknown. Indigenous mandate undefined.
  9. OCAP restrictions. Nation-level data restricted.
  10. Relational dimension missing. Designs are institutional.
  11. Mutual aid invisible. Community safety nets not mapped.
  12. Measurement gap. Limited Western evaluation of Indigenous programs = measurement gap, not effectiveness gap.
